Lines

Proposition. The line through a point with position vector a\mathbf{a}a and parallel to vector t\mathbf{t}t has equation x=a+λt\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda \mathbf{t}x=a+λt, where λ\lambdaλ is a constant.

Proof. If PPP denotes the point on the line, then the position vector of PPP OP⃗\vec{OP}OP equals OA⃗+AP⃗\vec{OA} + \vec{AP}OA+AP where OA⃗=a\vec{OA} = \mathbf{a}OA=a and AP⃗\vec{AP}AP is some vector in the direction of the line; hence, λt\lambda \mathbf{t}λt.

Proposition. The equation of the same line can also be rewritten (x−a)×t=0\mathbf{(x-a)\times t = 0}(x−a)×t=0 where ×\times× denotes the cross product. This can be intuitively seen because the only case when two vectors have a cross product of zero is if one is zero, or if they are parallel; thus either x=a\mathbf{x=a}x=a or x−a\mathbf{x-a}x−a is in the direction of t\mathbf{t}t.

Planes

Proposition. The equation of a plane perpendicular to some unit vector n\mathbf{n}n and passing through a point a\mathbf{a}a is (x−a)⋅n=0\mathbf{(x-a)\cdot n} = 0(x−a)⋅n=0. This can also be rewritten x⋅n=a⋅n\mathbf{x\cdot n = a\cdot n}x⋅n=a⋅n.

Vector equations

Equations involving vectors, the dot product and the cross product, such as x−(x×a)×b=c\mathbf{x - (x\times a)\times b = c}x−(x×a)×b=c, are often best solved through vector manipulation:

  1. To make a vector a\mathbf{a}a disappear on one side of the equation, take the cross product with a\mathbf{a}a on both sides, as a×a=0\mathbf{a\times a} = 0a×a=0.
  2. If two vectors are normal to one another, take their dot product.
  3. Use vector identities such as the vector triple product to expand more complicated expressions.
