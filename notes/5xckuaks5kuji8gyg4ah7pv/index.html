Tensors are Tensors

What are tensors?

Definition. A tensor is something that transforms like a tensor.

Perhaps one of the greatest distillations of human knowledge in written form ever to be recorded, alongside similar masterpieces such as "Newtonian physics are physics that make sense in a Newtonian frame of reference", "I would've won if I didn't lose!", and "risk of early-onset dementia declines sharply after the age of 70".

Just like how you could technically define a chair as being "a four-legged object somebody could sit on, excepting cows, pigs, sofas, and all non-chair things", a tensor is everything that looks like a tensor except for the things that aren't tensors. So what constitutes "looking like a tensor", and what constitutes the "except"?

What do tensors look like?

Just like how vectors look like a list of numbers or matrices look like a list of numbers in need of a weight-loss regime or Harry Potter looks like Daniel Radcliffe, tensors also look like a list of numbers: specifically, what we call an array, like a matrix or a vector - except this time it has more dimensions (three dimensions, or four, or a hundred), has more indices (TijkT_{ijk}Tijk​ instead of TijT_{ij}Tij​ or xix_ixi​), and is still a big list of numbers.

All tensors, therefore, can be represented as arrays: a group of numbers placed together in some nnn-dimensional grid. But are all arrays tensors? If you have an array, what excepts it from being a tensor?

What do tensors need to be?

What makes a vector a vector? You could just be tempted to say "a list of numbers", but that's not right - the list of numbers mean something specific: a point in space. A vector is a representation of a point in space.

Similarly, what makes a matrix a matrix? Basically every page of the however-many-hundred-pages-long course notes over in Linear Algebra was dedicated to stressing this: a matrix is not just an array of numbers, but a representation of a linear map - something that maps a point in Rn\mathbb{R^n}Rn to Rm\mathbb{R^m}Rm.

If vectors and matrices both represent something concrete, something that goes beyond the numbers that are present in the array itself, then it's reasonable to say that if we do something to that vector or that matrix - if we change its basis from Cartesian to whatever coordinate system we like, that underlying "something" - the point represented by a vector, or the linear map by a matrix, will remain exactly the same.

Essentially, that's what makes a tensor a tensor: invariance under changes of basis. A tensor represents something that doesn't change even in a different coordinate system (and as we'll see, that "something" is a multi-linear map between vector spaces), and do not depend on any set of particular coordinates: a constant of the universe, like π\piπ or eee or the speed of light.

Matrix transformations

As mentioned, matrices can be understood as representations of linear maps: for a vector x∈Rn\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R^n}x∈Rn and a n×nn \times nn×n matrix AAA, the matrix product

Ax=x′A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x'}Ax=x′

maps x\mathbf{x}x to a new vector x′∈Rn\mathbf{x'} \in \mathbb{R^n}x′∈Rn in a way that satisfies the conditions of linearity. A change of basis for a linear map is equivalent to asking this: if AAA took x\mathbf{x}x to x′\mathbf{x'}x′ with both being written in the basis ei\mathbf{e_i}ei​, what matrix A′A'A′ would take x\mathbf{x}x to x′\mathbf{x'}x′ in the basis ei′\mathbf{e_i'}ei′​?

To accomplish this, simply change basis for both x\mathbf{x}x and x′\mathbf{x'}x′ as per the vector transformation law (supposing that RRR is the transformation vector between bases ei\mathbf{e_i}ei​ and ei′\mathbf{e_i}'ei​′):

A′(Rx)=Rx′A'(R\mathbf{x}) = R\mathbf{x'}A′(Rx)=Rx′

with A′A'A′ being the matrix after a change of basis. Using x′=Ax\mathbf{x'} = A\mathbf{x}x′=Ax, we have

A′(Rx)=R(Ax)A' (R\mathbf{x}) = R(A\mathbf{x})A′(Rx)=R(Ax)

or A′R=RAA' R = RAA′R=RA, giving

A′=RAR−1=RARTA' = RAR^{-1} = RAR^{T}A′=RAR−1=RART

and in summation notation,

Theorem. Matrix transformation law. For a n×nn\times nn×n matrix AAA and a transformation matrix RRR between two bases ei\mathbf{e_i}ei​ and ei′\mathbf{e_i'}ei′​, we have

Aij′=RikAklRljT=RikRjlAkl.A'_{ij} = R_{ik}A_{kl}R^T_{lj} = R_{ik}R_{jl}A_{kl}.Aij′​=Rik​Akl​RljT​=Rik​Rjl​Akl​.

with A′A'A′ being the matrix under a change of basis by RRR.

Note that - crucially - the matrix changes under a change of basis, but not the linear map itself.

What is not a tensor?

This is a tensor. We know that because it's a square, and it has loads of numbers in it, and it even has that funny little thing at the bottom we call an index. That means it has to be a tensor, right? Right?

WRONG, you stupid bonehead. It's not. Or at least, we don't know if it is.

If we say a vector is a tensor or a matrix is a tensor, what does that mean, exactly? We aren't saying that the list of numbers making up the vector is a tensor; we're saying that the point the vector describes is a tensor, because it doesn't change under a change of basis. We aren't saying that the array making up a matrix is a tensor either; we're saying that the linear map the matrix represents is a tensor, because that doesn't change under a change of basis.

Therefore, it's crucial that we all repeat after me: just like how a vector represents a point and a matrix a linear map, a tensor is not an array of numbers, but the multilinear map that array of numbers represents. Therefore, if a real number doesn't describe a quantity that's invariant under change of basis, or if a vector doesn't describe the same point, or if a matrix doesn't describe a linear map, then they aren't tensors. Is A=3A = 3A=3 a tensor? If AAA means area, then it isn't - area depends on the basis used. Again, is

a tensor? If it's a linear map, sure, but if the numbers mean "number of ants crawling on each square of my 3×33\times 33×3 chocolate bar" then probably not.

Proposition. One final note: both the Levi-Civita symbol ϵijk\epsilon_{ijk}ϵijk​ and the Kronecker delta δij\delta_{ij}δij​ are tensors. They have the special property of being a little baby and not changing their components even when everyone else is changing their basis. Call these tensors invariant tensors (more on this later!)
