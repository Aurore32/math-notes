Singular Value Decomposition

Defining SVD

Definition. A singular value decomposition is a factorization of a real or complex m×nm\times nm×n matrix AAA, of the form

A=UΣV†A = U \Sigma V^{\dagger}A=UΣV†

where UUU is a m×mm\times mm×m unitary matrix, Σ\SigmaΣ is a m×nm\times nm×n rectangular diagonal matrix with non-negative real numbers on the diagonal, and VVV is a n×nn\times nn×n unitary matrix.

Definition. In the above factorization, we typically denote the diagonal terms of Σ\SigmaΣ, σ1,σ2,...,σn\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_nσ1​,σ2​,...,σn​, as the singular values of AAA (usually reordered so that σ1≥σ2≥...≥σn\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq ... \geq \sigma_nσ1​≥σ2​≥...≥σn​).

This can be thought of as a generalization of the diagonalization

A=PΛP−1A = P\Lambda P^{-1}A=PΛP−1

applicable only for square matrices that have nnn linearly independent eigenvectors.

Constructing a possible SVD

Theorem. Every real or complex m×nm\times nm×n matrix AAA has a singular value decomposition.

Proof. We prove the above statement by literally willing the factorization into existence through the power of God and anime Hermitian matrices on our side. This is considered to be a "sigma move", and - this is true - is the reason why Σ\SigmaΣ is the letter chosen for SVD.

Begin by defining

Definition. A square matrix HHH is positive semi-definite if it has non-negative eigenvalues; a matrix DDD is positive definite if it has strictly positive eigenvalues.

Sketch of proof.

  1. Construct a Hermitian matrix H=A†AH = A^{\dagger}AH=A†A and prove it is positive semi-definite using the Hermitian form.
  2. Denote the eigenvalues of HHH, all non-negative by virtue of semi-definiteness, as λ1,...,λn\lambda_1, ..., \lambda_nλ1​,...,λn​, with corresponding eigenvectors v1,v2,...,vn\mathbf{v_1, v_2, ..., v_n}v1​,v2​,...,vn​.
  3. Construct another Hermitian matrix H′=AA†H' = AA^{\dagger}H′=AA†. Show that AviA\mathbf{v_i}Avi​ are eigenvectors of H′H'H′, and that H′H'H′ has the same nonzero eigenvalues as HHH.
  4. Using the fact that transformation matrices from the standard orthonormal basis to the orthonormal eigenvectors of a Hermitian matrix are unitary, construct the SVD.

What if we have some eigenvalues equal to zero?

In that case, we will no longer be able to find

Suppose that σ1,σ2,...,σr\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_rσ1​,σ2​,...,σr​ are strictly positive after sorting, and that σr+1,...,σn\sigma_{r+1}, ..., \sigma_nσr+1​,...,σn​ are all zero. Obtain u1,u2,...,ur\mathbf{u_1, u_2, ..., u_r}u1​,u2​,...,ur​ as above. By the Gram-Schmidt process, it is always possible to find w1,...,wn−r\mathbf{w_{1}, ..., w_{n-r}}w1​,...,wn−r​ such that

{u1,u2,...,ur,w1,...,wn−r}\{\mathbf{u_1, u_2, ..., u_r, w_1, ..., w_{n-r}}\}{u1​,u2​,...,ur​,w1​,...,wn−r​}

where Σ\SigmaΣ is a square matrix with the singular values σi\sigma_iσi​ on its diagonal, as σr+1,...,σn\sigma_{r+1}, ..., \sigma_nσr+1​,...,σn​ are zero and make Σ\SigmaΣ non-invertible; instead, we write directly

where Σ\SigmaΣ has rows equal to zero corresponding to the columns w1,...,wn−r\mathbf{w_1}, ..., \mathbf{w_{n-r}}w1​,...,wn−r​ of UUU.

Definition. The mmm columns of UUU are referred to as the left-singular vectors of AAA; the nnn columns of VVV are referred to as the right-singular vectors.

Interpretations of the SVD

Geometric interpretation.

The SVD decomposes any m×nm\times nm×n matrix AAA, itself representing a linear map from Rn→Rm\mathbb{R^n \to R^m}Rn→Rm, into

A=UΣV−1A = U\Sigma V^{-1}A=UΣV−1

or three linear maps V−1,ΣV^{-1}, \SigmaV−1,Σ, and UUU applied in that order, where Σ\SigmaΣ is diagonal and UUU and VVV are unitary. Unitary matrices (and their inverses) map one orthonormal basis to another, and thus they can be thought of as rotations or reflections: they "rotate" or "reflect" the original basis, sometimes in combination, to form the new one. Thus, the SVD tells us that any linear map A:Rn→RmA: \mathbb{R^n \to R^m}A:Rn→Rm is equivalent to three maps applied in sequence:

  1. The rotation/reflection matrix V−1V^{-1}V−1.
  2. The enlargement matrix Σ\SigmaΣ, which enlarges the vector in some directions by a scalar multiple and may also make the other directions disappear (as Σ\SigmaΣ maps from Rn→Rm\mathbb{R^n \to R^m}Rn→Rm.)
  3. The rotation/reflection matrix UUU.

Note that the notion of a "rotation/reflection" only really makes sense if UUU and VVV are real, i.e. they are orthogonal instead of unitary.

Series interpretation.

The SVD can be thought of as a matrix analogue of functional series like the Taylor or Fourier series. To observe this, note that

A=[u1u2...um][σ100...0σ20...00σ3...⋮⋮⋮⋱000…][v1†v2†⋮vn†]A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{u_1} & \mathbf{u_2} & ... & \mathbf{u_m} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & 0 & ... \\ 0 & \sigma_2 & 0 & ... \\ 0 & 0 & \sigma_3 & ... \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{v_1^\dagger} \\ \mathbf{v_2^\dagger} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{v_n^\dagger} \end{bmatrix}A=[u1​​u2​​...​um​​]⎣⎡​σ1​00⋮0​0σ2​0⋮0​00σ3​⋮0​.........⋱…​⎦⎤​⎣⎡​v1†​v2†​⋮vn†​​⎦⎤​

Which leads to

A=[u1u2...um][σ1v1†σ2v2†⋮]A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{u_1} & \mathbf{u_2} & ... & \mathbf{u_m} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1\mathbf{v_1^\dagger} \\ \sigma_2\mathbf{v_2^\dagger} \\ \vdots \\ \end{bmatrix}A=[u1​​u2​​...​um​​]⎣⎡​σ1​v1†​σ2​v2†​⋮​⎦⎤​

and

each of which are simpler m×nm\times nm×n matrices, with ui\mathbf{u_i}ui​ being m×1m \times 1m×1 and vi†\mathbf{v_i}^\daggervi​† being 1×n1 \times n1×n. This is useful in contexts such as image decomposition, where a large image stored as a matrix of pixels can be decomposed into simpler incomplete images.

Applications of SVD

Application. Linear least-squares.

Suppose we are trying to fit a multivariate linear regression model to data: for some input in nnn variables

x=[x1x2⋮xn],\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix},x=⎣⎡​x1​x2​⋮xn​​⎦⎤​,

and some output we want in mmm variables (most commonly just a single variable)

y=[y1y2⋮ym],\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_m \end{bmatrix},y=⎣⎡​y1​y2​⋮ym​​⎦⎤​,

can we find a m×nm \times nm×n matrix WWW such that the systems of linear equations

Wx=[W11x1+W12x2+...+W1nxnW21x1+W22x2+...+W2nxn⋮Wn1x1+Wn2x2+...+Wnnxn]W\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} W_{11}x_1 + W_{12}x_2 + ... + W_{1n}x_n \\ W_{21}x_1 + W_{22}x_2 + ... + W_{2n}x_n \\ \vdots \\ W_{n1}x_1 + W_{n2}x_2 + ... + W_{nn}x_n \end{bmatrix}Wx=⎣⎡​W11​x1​+W12​x2​+...+W1n​xn​W21​x1​+W22​x2​+...+W2n​xn​⋮Wn1​x1​+Wn2​x2​+...+Wnn​xn​​⎦⎤​

This is the problem of linear least-squares; if solved correctly, it gives us the line of best fit for a set of data, perfect for all your linear regression needs such as predicting the Earth's climate in 2050, mapping your level of respect in me as a human being over time, and forecasting how many chicks you'll pull per year in the next decade (perfectly modeled by a flat line). It is slightly less perfect for modelling needs such as trying to "conquer the stock market" (sorry, Chad, I don't think you're going to become the next Warren Buffett using Excel), tracking my erotic fascination towards Danny DeVito over time (exponential growth), or calculating the gravitational pull between me and your mom.
