The univariate chain rule, revisited

Definition. A composite function (of one variable) is a function of the form f(x)f(x)f(x) where xxx is itself a function of another variable ttt: x=g(t)x = g(t)x=g(t).

The chain rule in 1D aims to answer the question: if fff depends on xxx and xxx depends on ttt, how does fff change when ttt change? - i.e. what is dfdt\frac{df}{dt}dtdf​? The two ways of notating the result are either

dfdt=f′(g(t))g′(t)\frac{df}{dt} = f'(g(t))g'(t)dtdf​=f′(g(t))g′(t)

or

dfdt=dfdxdxdt\frac{df}{dt} = \frac{df}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}dtdf​=dxdf​dtdx​

but they speak the same meaning: the rate of change of fff with respect to ttt is the rate of change of fff with respect to xxx, multiplied by the rate of change of xxx with respect to ttt.

Caution. You will use the second notation, and if you use the first notation, I will personally call 911 and notify the Cringe Police of England that it's cringe-o-clock on your sorry ass.

You may have also heard a variety of analogies to justify this statement, each more sensible and life-changing than the last: e.g. "to turn the second gear you have to turn the first", "to eat a M&M you eat the chocolate shell before you eat the filling", "to microwave a pregnant mother you microwave the mother with the baby inside then microwave the baby", etc., etc.

The chain rule in 2D

Now consider a function of two variables

z=f(x,y)z = f(x,y)z=f(x,y)

where each of x,yx, yx,y are functions of ttt:

f(x,y)=f(g(t),h(t)).f(x,y) = f(g(t), h(t)).f(x,y)=f(g(t),h(t)).
