Gradients

Gradients are denoted by ∇\nabla∇, pronounced "del" or "nabla" because they look like an ancient harp-like Hebrew instrument with the same shape. This is astonishing, as the list of ∇\nabla∇-shaped objects has length N>>1N >> 1N>>1 and some of them roll off the tongue much better, like "Huell Babineaux's pointy head hanging upside-down on a yoga mat" and "Danny DeVito's humongous dumptruck".

The Gradient Vector

Definition. The gradient of a multivariate scalar function f(x1,x2,...,xm):Rm→Rf(x_1, x_2, ..., x_m): \mathbb{R^m \to R}f(x1​,x2​,...,xm​):Rm→R, denoted ∇f\nabla f∇f and pronounced REDACTED, is defined as the vector

∇f=[∂f∂x1∂f∂x2⋮∂f∂xn]\nabla f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix}∇f=⎣⎡​∂x1​∂f​∂x2​∂f​⋮∂xn​∂f​​⎦⎤​

i.e. a vector whose components are the partial derivatives of fff in sequence.

Properties. From the properties of (partial) differentiation, and if fff and ggg are both functions mapping from Rm→R\mathbb{R^m \to R}Rm→R.n:

  1. ∇(λf+μg)=λ∇f+μ∇g\nabla(\lambda f + \mu g) = \lambda \nabla f + \mu \nabla g∇(λf+μg)=λ∇f+μ∇g;
  2. ∇(fg)=f∇g+g∇f.\nabla(fg) = f\nabla g + g \nabla f.∇(fg)=f∇g+g∇f.

Theorem. The directional derivative of fff in the direction of the unit vector u^\hat{u}u^ is given by the dot product between the gradient vector and u^\hat{u}u^, (∇f)⋅u^(\nabla f) \cdot \hat{u}(∇f)⋅u^. Simultaneously, the direction of steepest ascent - the direction where the function increases the most - is given by the gradient vector itself.

Proof.

Recall that the directional derivative of fff in the direction of u^\hat{u}u^ is the change of fff when taking an infinitesimally small step in the direction u^\hat{u}u^. Thus if

u^=[u1u2⋮um]\hat{u} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_m \end{bmatrix}u^=⎣⎡​u1​u2​⋮um​​⎦⎤​

i.e. u1u_1u1​ steps in direction x1x_1x1​, u2u_2u2​ in x2x_2x2​, etc., and the rate of change in each of these directions are given by ∂f∂x1,∂f∂x2\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}∂x1​∂f​,∂x2​∂f​, etc., we have

f′(x;u^)=u1∂f∂x1+u2∂f∂x2+...+um∂f∂xm=∇f⋅u^.f'(\mathbf{x}; \hat{u}) = u_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} + u_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} + ... + u_m \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_m} = \nabla{f} \cdot \hat{u}.f′(x;u^)=u1​∂x1​∂f​+u2​∂x2​∂f​+...+um​∂xm​∂f​=∇f⋅u^.

What is the maximum value of such a directional derivative, i.e. what direction does fff increase the quickest in? ∇f⋅u^\nabla{f}\cdot \hat{u}∇f⋅u^ has magnitude at most ∇f\nabla{f}∇f as u^\hat{u}u^ is a unit vector, and this maximum is achieved when ∇f\nabla{f}∇f and u^\hat{u}u^ are in the same direction (the cosine of the angle in-between them is 111). Thus, when you travel in the direction of the gradient vector, you ascend the function fff quickest.
