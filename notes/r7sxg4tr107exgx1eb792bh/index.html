Divergence Theorem

The Divergence Theorem comes full circle.

A new understanding of divergence

Profile: Carl Freidrich Johann Heinrich Kaiser Wilheim Ludwig Beethoven von Gauss.



Gauss calculated the precise value of ∑i=1100i\sum_{i=1}^{100} i∑i=1100​i in three picoseconds while still an unborn fetus. He is best known for being the namesake to 100 concepts, 7 billion theorems, and my firstborn child, and for since ascending to divinity as eternal patron saint of all mathematicians worldwide.

The Divergence Theorem, also known as Gauss' Theorem, is stated as follows:

Theorem. The Divergence Theorem. For a smooth vector field F(x)\mathbf{F(x)}F(x) defined over R3\mathbb{R^3}R3, as well as a bounded region VVV and its associated piecewise-smooth boundary ∂V=S\partial V = S∂V=S, we have

∫V∇⋅F dV=∫SF⋅dS.\int_{V}\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV = \int_{S} \mathbf{F \cdot dS}.∫V​∇⋅F dV=∫S​F⋅dS.

(where the direction of dS\mathbf{dS}dS is out of the boundary.)

Building intuition for the Divergence Theorem

Why I season integrate over my cutting board surface, NOT my steak volume

Our rudimentary understanding of divergence is that of a measure of flow. (This is admittedly putting Descartes before the horse, because this rudimentary understanding exists only because of the Divergence Theorem.) A positive divergence at a point indicates that the vector field has a net-flow into the point; a negative divergence indicates a net flow out of the point.

If divergence quantifies flow at very point, then what does the left-hand side of the Divergence Theorem

∫V∇⋅F dV,\int_V \nabla\cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV,∫V​∇⋅F dV,

a volume integral of the divergence of F\mathbf{F}F over the entire region VVV, mean? Intuitively, this is the sum of the net flow of F\mathbf{F}F at every point in VVV - the total net flow of the field inside the region. Meanwhile, the surface integral on the right-hand side

∫SF⋅dS\int_S \mathbf{F \cdot dS}∫S​F⋅dS

measures something very particular: the flux of the vector field out of the surface SSS which bounds the region VVV - in essence, the net flow of F\mathbf{F}F on the outside/exterior of VVV.

Taken together, the Divergence Theorem states simply thus: the net amount of F\mathbf{F}F flowing outward from the inside of a region VVV equals the amount of F\mathbf{F}F that actually flows outward through its surface. Or, better yet:

If you got gas in your belly, you better believe you're gonna pass that gas out the other side, yo.

Re-defining divergence

Divergence measures the net flow of a vector field into or out of a given point - but why? The divergence theorem itself provides us with the essential clues.

Consider an infinitesimal region VVV that encloses exactly a single point x\mathbf{x}x in space - a region approaching zero volume. The boundary surface SSS enclosing that region is the point itself; the volume integral of the divergence of a vector field F\mathbf{F}F throughout that region

∫V∇⋅F dV\int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV∫V​∇⋅F dV

is no more than the divergence ∇⋅F\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}∇⋅F at x\mathbf{x}x, multiplied by the volume VVV of the region:

∫V∇⋅F dV=V(∇⋅F)(x)\int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV = V(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})(\mathbf{x})∫V​∇⋅F dV=V(∇⋅F)(x)

as, at infinitesimal scales, the divergence ∇⋅F\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}∇⋅F is roughly constant.

On the other hand, the Divergence Theorem gives us

∫SF⋅dS\int_S \mathbf{F \cdot dS}∫S​F⋅dS

leading to

Definition. Coordinate-free definition of divergence. For a vector field F:R3→R3\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R^3 \to R^3}F:R3→R3, the Divergence Theorem about an infinitesimal region VVV enclosing some point x∈R3\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R^3}x∈R3 gives

∇⋅F=lim⁡V→0∫SF⋅dSV.\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \lim_{V\to 0}\frac{\int_S \mathbf{F\cdot dS}}{V}.∇⋅F=V→0lim​V∫S​F⋅dS​.

At such infinitesimal scales, ∫SF⋅dS\int_S \mathbf{F\cdot dS}∫S​F⋅dS is not only the flux of F\mathbf{F}F out of the surface SSS, but - as SSS is the point x\mathbf{x}x itself - also just the flow of F\mathbf{F}F out of the point x\mathbf{x}x. This ultimately gives rise to our interpretation of divergence as flow: for instance, Maxwell's equations state that

∇⋅B=0\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0∇⋅B=0

indicating a magnetic field that does not contain any sources or sinks and flows in and out of every point at equal measure, and

∇⋅E=ρϵ0\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}∇⋅E=ϵ0​ρ​

with ρ\rhoρ being charge density, indicating that electric field gathers/flows out of points where charge is densely collected.

The Divergence Theroem in scalar fields

Theorem. (Divergence theorem in scalar fields). For a region VVV in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 and its boundary surface S=∂VS = \partial VS=∂V, along with a smooth scalar field ϕ\phiϕ in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3, we have

∫V∇ϕ dV=∫Sϕ dS.\int_V \nabla \phi\ dV = \int_S \phi\ d\mathbf{S}.∫V​∇ϕ dV=∫S​ϕ dS.

Proof.

This arises from a special case of the (general) Divergence Theorem: take F=aϕ\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{a}\phiF=aϕ for a constant vector a\mathbf{a}a, with

∫V∇⋅F dV=∫V∇⋅(aϕ) dV=∫Va∇ϕ dV=∫Sϕ(a⋅dS)\int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV = \int_V \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{a}\phi)\ dV = \int_V \mathbf{a}\nabla\phi\ dV = \int_S\phi (\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{dS})∫V​∇⋅F dV=∫V​∇⋅(aϕ) dV=∫V​a∇ϕ dV=∫S​ϕ(a⋅dS)

by the Divergence Theorem, leading to

∫V∇ϕ dV=∫Sϕ dS.\int_V \nabla \phi\ dV = \int_S \phi\ d\mathbf{S}.∫V​∇ϕ dV=∫S​ϕ dS.

Sketching the proof

Attempt 1

Picasso would be spinning in his grave.

Suppose that we divide a region VVV in 3D space into an infinite number of infinitesimal cubes, denoted VxV_{\mathbf{x}}Vx​; we want to claim that these cubes added together will exactly equal VVV. Sadly, this is extremely suspect; no matter how small the cubes become, there will always be "gaps" between the cube and the actual region that remain nonzero even when the cubes have zero volume. This is called the staircase paradox, e.g.

whose natural conclusion would suggest π=4\pi = 4π=4. In engineering, this statement is known as "the Engineer's Approximation"; in math, it's known as "a really bad idea."

So with the foreknowledge that this does not work, and in the parallel universe where it did work, π\piπ also equals 444 and 2\sqrt{2}2​ equals 111 and I still managed to "befriend" your mom last night, let us sally forth.

Pictured above is one of the infinitesimal cubes VxV_xVx​, along with a vector field F\mathbf{F}F. Consider the Divergence Theorem for this miniature example: does

∫Vx∇⋅F dV=∫SxF⋅dS\int_{V_x} \nabla\cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV = \int_{S_x} \mathbf{F\cdot dS}∫Vx​​∇⋅F dV=∫Sx​​F⋅dS

hold, with the flux being evaluated along the six sides of the cube?

Let's consider flux first. The cube has three pairs of opposite sides, which, in turn, have opposite normal vectors; consider, for example, the two sides placed in the (y,z)(y,z)(y,z) plane, with ex=±(1,0,0)e_x = \pm(1,0,0)ex​=±(1,0,0) as their normal vector(s). This results in

which is

[Fx(x+δx,y,z)−Fx(x,y,z)] δy δz[\mathbf{F}_x(x+\delta x, y, z) - \mathbf{F}_x(x,y,z)]\ \delta y\ \delta z[Fx​(x+δx,y,z)−Fx​(x,y,z)] δy δz

rewritable in terms of partial derivatives and linear approximations as

∂Fx∂x δx δy δz.\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}_x}{\partial x} \ \delta x\ \delta y\ \delta z.∂x∂Fx​​ δx δy δz.

Similarly, out of the pair of faces in the xzxzxz-plane, we have

∂Fy∂y δx δy δz;\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}_y}{\partial y} \ \delta x\ \delta y\ \delta z;∂y∂Fy​​ δx δy δz;

∂Fz∂z δx δy δz.\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}_z}{\partial z} \ \delta x\ \delta y\ \delta z.∂z∂Fz​​ δx δy δz.

In total, therefore, the flux through the surface of the cube is

∫SxF⋅dS=(∂Fx∂x+∂Fy∂y+∂Fz∂z) δx δy δz=(∇⋅F) δV\int_{S_x} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = (\frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z})\ \delta x\ \delta y\ \delta z = (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\ \delta V∫Sx​​F⋅dS=(∂x∂Fx​​+∂y∂Fy​​+∂z∂Fz​​) δx δy δz=(∇⋅F) δV

which is by definition equal to

∫Vx∇⋅F dV\int_{V_x} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV∫Vx​​∇⋅F dV

as ∇⋅F\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}∇⋅F is constant over an infinitesimal range and the volume integral is thus a constant multiplied by the volume dV=dx dy dzdV = dx\ dy\ dzdV=dx dy dz.

If the divergence theorem holds for an infinitesimal cube, does it hold for the entire region VVV? Let's now consider

∫V∇⋅F dV\int_{V} \nabla\cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV∫V​∇⋅F dV

over the entire volume VVV, which - by our assumption that all the little cubes add up to VVV - is equal to the volume integral for each little cube VxV_xVx​ added together:

∫V∇⋅F dV=∑x∫Vx∇⋅F dV\int_{V} \nabla\cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV = \sum_x \int_{V_x} \nabla\cdot \mathbf{F}\ dV∫V​∇⋅F dV=x∑​∫Vx​​∇⋅F dV

But as the divergence theorem holds for these little cubes, the above is equivalent to

∑x∫SxF⋅dS\sum_x \int_{S_x} \mathbf{F\cdot dS}x∑​∫Sx​​F⋅dS

also. Our last task is to prove that this sum of infinitesimal surface integrals is equal to the bigger surface integral over the entire boundary surface SSS,

∫SF⋅dS.\int_S \mathbf{F \cdot dS}.∫S​F⋅dS.

To accomplish this, consider the following picture.

Any two faces that do not lie directly on the boundary SSS of VVV will touch. Along these touching faces, the value of F\mathbf{F}F is identical - but the normal vector dS\mathbf{dS}dS is equal and opposite in direction. The surface integrals along these two faces thus cancel out; and along the next pair of touching faces, it, too, cancels out; until all that is left is the surface integral along the faces that form the boundary SSS, i.e.

∑x∫SxF⋅dS=∫SF⋅dS.\sum_x \int_{S_x} \mathbf{F\cdot dS} = \int_S \mathbf{F \cdot dS}.x∑​∫Sx​​F⋅dS=∫S​F⋅dS.

Attempt 2-D

A lot of things in life would be easier if we could reduce them to two dimensions. Like proving the divergence theorem. Or relationships.

The glaring issue we want to amend in our previous proof is the way in which we sub-divided the region: dividing a region into infinitesimal cubes does not yield a "smooth" approximation whose difference with the actual region vanishes as the subdivision gets finer. Instead of parameterizing a 3D region into cubes - or a 2D region into squares - we want to work with parallelograms and angled lines.

To make this concept a bit clearer, we consider

Theorem. The Divergence Theorem, 2D. The anime adaptation of the famed Hollywood live-action blockbuster. For a two-dimensional region DDD and its bounding curve CCC, as well as a vector field in R2\mathbb{R^2}R2 denoted F\mathbf{F}F, we have

∫D∇⋅F dA=∫CF⋅n ds.\int_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dA = \int_C \mathbf{F\cdot n}\ ds.∫D​∇⋅F dA=∫C​F⋅n ds.

Note that the right-hand side is not the usual line integral defined for vector fields; the dot product is with the normal vector to the curve, and not the tangent vector.

Proof.

The big idea: consider the xxx-component and yyy-component of F\mathbf{F}F separately. Divergence of a single component is a single partial derivative. Area integral of the partial derivative is a line integral along the boundary.

We begin with

F=Fx(x,y)e1+Fy(x,y)e2.\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_x(x,y)\mathbf{e_1} + \mathbf{F}_y(x,y)\mathbf{e_2}.F=Fx​(x,y)e1​+Fy​(x,y)e2​.

If the above statement is valid for the xxx- and yyy-components separately, then it is valid for the general vector field F\mathbf{F}F due to the linearity of integration.

First consider the yyy-component of the vector field in vector form, F⃗y(x,y)=(0,Fy)\mathbf{\vec{F}}_y(x,y) = (0, F_y)Fy​(x,y)=(0,Fy​). Beginning with the left-hand side:

∫D∇⋅Fy dA=∫D(∂x,∂y)⋅(0,Fy) dy dx=∫X∫y−(x)y+(x)∂yFy dy dx=∫XFy(x,y+(x))−Fy(x,y−(x)) dx\begin{aligned} \int_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}_y\ dA &= \int_D (\partial_x, \partial_y) \cdot (0, F_y)\ dy\ dx \\ &= \int_{X}\int_{y_-(x)}^{y_+(x)}\partial_y F_y\ dy\ dx \\ &= \int_X F_y(x, y_+(x)) - F_y(x, y_-(x))\ dx \end{aligned}∫D​∇⋅Fy​ dA​=∫D​(∂x​,∂y​)⋅(0,Fy​) dy dx=∫X​∫y−​(x)y+​(x)​∂y​Fy​ dy dx=∫X​Fy​(x,y+​(x))−Fy​(x,y−​(x)) dx​

by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

To convert this to the line integral we desire, we ponder on the relationship between dxdxdx and dsdsds. Suppose that the unit normal to the curve CCC is denoted n\mathbf{n}n; then the angle θ\thetaθ formed by n\mathbf{n}n and the yyy-axis is given by

cos⁡θ=n⋅y^\cos \theta = \mathbf{n} \cdot \hat{y}cosθ=n⋅y^​

for the upward-pointing portion of the curve C+C_+C+​, and

cos⁡θ=−n⋅y^\cos \theta = -\mathbf{n} \cdot \hat{y}cosθ=−n⋅y^​

for the downward-pointing portion C−C_-C−​, where y^\hat{y}y^​ is the unit vector in the yyy-direction. θ\thetaθ is also the angle formed by the xxx-axis and the tangent to the curve dsdsds:

where we now have

δx=δscos⁡θ=±δs(n⋅y^)\delta x = \delta s \cos \theta = \pm \delta s (\mathbf{n\cdot\hat{y}})δx=δscosθ=±δs(n⋅y^​)

depending on if we are on C+C_+C+​ or C−C_-C−​. In any case, we have

F⃗y(x,y)⋅n=(0,Fy)⋅n=Fy(x,y)(n⋅y^)\vec{F}_y(x,y) \cdot \mathbf{n} = (0, F_y)\cdot \mathbf{n} = F_y(x,y) (\mathbf{n} \cdot \hat{y})Fy​(x,y)⋅n=(0,Fy​)⋅n=Fy​(x,y)(n⋅y^​)

Substituting back into the area integral, we have

∫XFy⃗(x,y+(x))−Fy⃗(x,y−(x)) dx=∫XFy(x,y+(x))−Fy(x,y−(x)) (n⋅y^) ds=∫X(Fy⃗(x,y+(x))⋅n) ds+(F⃗y(x,y−(x))⋅n) ds(as y+ lies on C+ and y− lies on C−)=∫C−F⃗y ds+∫C+F⃗y ds=∫CFy⃗⋅n ds\begin{aligned} \int_X \vec{F_y}(x, y_+(x)) - \vec{F_y}(x, y_-(x))\ dx &= \int_X F_y(x, y_+(x)) - F_y(x, y_-(x))\ (\mathbf{n \cdot \hat{y}})\ ds \\ &= \int_X (\vec{F_y}(x, y_+ (x))\cdot \mathbf{n})\ ds + (\vec{F}_y(x, y_- (x))\cdot\mathbf{n})\ ds \\ &\text{(as $y_+$ lies on $C_+$ and $y_-$ lies on $C_-$)} \\ &= \int_{C_-}\vec{F}_y\ ds + \int_{C_+} \vec{F}_y\ ds \\ &= \int_C \vec{F_y}\cdot \mathbf{n}\ ds \end{aligned}∫X​Fy​​(x,y+​(x))−Fy​​(x,y−​(x)) dx​=∫X​Fy​(x,y+​(x))−Fy​(x,y−​(x)) (n⋅y^​) ds=∫X​(Fy​​(x,y+​(x))⋅n) ds+(Fy​(x,y−​(x))⋅n) ds(as y+​ lies on C+​ and y−​ lies on C−​)=∫C−​​Fy​ ds+∫C+​​Fy​ ds=∫C​Fy​​⋅n ds​

∫D∇⋅F dA=∫∂DF⋅n ds\int_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\ dA = \int_{\partial D}\mathbf{F \cdot n}\ ds∫D​∇⋅F dA=∫∂D​F⋅n ds

Proving the Divergence Theorem is like losing your virginity: the third time's the charm.

The big idea: consider the xxx-, yyy-, and zzz-components separately. Divergence of a single component is a partial derivative. Volume integral of partial derivative is surface integral along boundary.

Proof.

Suppose, as above, that we may write

F=Fx⃗+Fy⃗+Fz⃗\mathbf{F} = \vec{F_x} + \vec{F_y} + \vec{F_z}F=Fx​​+Fy​​+Fz​​
