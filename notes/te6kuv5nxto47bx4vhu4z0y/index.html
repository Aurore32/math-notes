Orbits and stabilizers represent analogues to a function's range and its fixed-points for group actions. In particular, the two relate together in a way that gives us insight into the sizes of groups that act on particular sets.

Now here's the big one. The larger the stablizer of an element x∈Xx \in Xx∈X, the lower the number of elements g∈Gg \in Gg∈G that can move it around via the group action; and the lower the number of elements that move xxx around, the lower the number of possibilities for where xxx can be moved - i.e. the size of the orbit of xxx. As such, we expect that the size of the orbit is "inversely proportional" - in some vague, informal sense - to the size of the stabilizer; and indeed

Theorem. (THE BIG ONE.) Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem, named after co-discoverers John Orbit and Edward Stabilizer in 1887. Let a group GGG act on a set XXX by means of a group action. Then the size of the orbit of an element x∈Xx \in Xx∈X under that group action multiplied by the size of the stabilizer of xxx is the size of GGG:

∣orb(x)∣∣stab(x)∣=∣G∣.|\text{orb}(x)||\text{stab}(x)| = |G|.∣orb(x)∣∣stab(x)∣=∣G∣.
