Evaluating Surface Integrals

Generalizing the line integral

Since we're studying all these different types of integrals, and each one is a generalization or specification or manifestation or personification of the last, it's occasionally useful to understand exactly how they're all connected - and what parts they're generalizing off of one another.

A surface integral is a generalization of two types of integrals: an area integral, whose idea of integrating along infinitesimal areas dAdAdA is generalized to two-dimensional surfaces in three dimensions, rather than in the two-dimensional plane, and a line integral, whose idea of taking directional slices along a curve is generalized to taking directional slices along a surface.

Let's take a look at how each of these generalizations operate.

Areas along a parametric surface

For an area integral, the area differential dA=dx dydA = dx\ dydA=dx dy represented the process of splitting a region into infinitesimally small rectangular areas, each with length dxdxdx and with dydydy. For a surface integral over a parametric surface S=x(u,v)S = \mathbf{x}(u,v)S=x(u,v), we deal not with rectangular areas, but with surface areas along the uuu- and vvv-directions - much like how, over a curve CCC, we deal with arc length dsdsds instead of the differential dxdxdx along a straight line.

There is zero guarantee that uuu and vvv are orthogonal; given a small increment along the uuu-axis δu\delta uδu and along the vvv-axis δv\delta vδv, we can only guarantee that, at infinitesimal scales, the infinite sum of the parallelograms formed by the vector

x(u+δu,v)−x(u,v)\mathbf{x}(u+\delta u, v) - \mathbf{x}(u, v)x(u+δu,v)−x(u,v)

and the vector

x(u,v+δv)−x(u,v)\mathbf{x}(u, v+\delta v) - \mathbf{x}(u,v)x(u,v+δv)−x(u,v)

can approximate the surface area of SSS. The above two expressions look exactly like partial derivatives:

x(u+δu,v)−x(u,v)=∂x∂u δu\mathbf{x}(u+\delta u, v) - \mathbf{x}(u, v) = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial u} \ \delta ux(u+δu,v)−x(u,v)=∂u∂x​ δu

and, correspondingly,

x(u,v+δv)−x(u,v)=∂x∂v δv.\mathbf{x}(u, v+\delta v) - \mathbf{x}(u, v) = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial v} \ \delta v.x(u,v+δv)−x(u,v)=∂v∂x​ δv.

The area of the parallelogram formed by two vectors is the magnitude of the cross-product of the two vectors, and thus our expression for the surface area differential dSdSdS is

∣∂x∂u δu×∂x∂v δv∣| \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial u} \ \delta u \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial v} \ \delta v|∣∂u∂x​ δu×∂v∂x​ δv∣

Vector fields

As promised, we now define the vector-valued analogue to the scalar area differential dSdSdS:

Definition. Define the vector area differential, denoted dSd\mathbf{S}dS, for a surface SSS parameterized by x(u,v)\mathbf{x}(u,v)x(u,v) as

dS=(∂x∂u×∂x∂v) du dvd\mathbf{S} = (\frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial u}\times \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial v})\ du\ dvdS=(∂u∂x​×∂v∂x​) du dv

with the cross-product term no longer behind bars, having served its sentence for first-degree murder of kxk\mathbf{x}kx in the presence of x\mathbf{x}x. In contrast to dSdSdS, dSd\mathbf{S}dS is a vector with direction pointing in the normal to the surface and magnitude dSdSdS; it thus encodes both surface area and surface direction within it, and that fact allows us to calculate

Definition. Flux. Suppose that the vector field F(x)\mathbf{F(x)}F(x) denotes the velocity field of a fluid, i.e. its direction and magnitude of flow at every point; we define the flux of the fluid about a surface SSS as the amount of fluid crossing the surface SSS per unit time.

This definition of flux is made calculable through the surface integral by two relevant facts:

  1. The direction pointing out of the surface at a point is given by dSd\mathbf{S}dS (pointing in the direction of the normal vector to the surface);
  2. The flow of a fluid F\mathbf{F}F in the direction n\mathbf{n}n is given by the dot product F⋅n\mathbf{F\cdot n}F⋅n.

All of this leads to the following formulation for flux, given a surface SSS, a velocity field F(x)\mathbf{F(x)}F(x), and a region DDD in the u−vu-vu−v plane over which the surface lies:

∫DF⋅dS\int_D \mathbf{F\cdot dS}∫D​F⋅dS

We can, of course, speak of flux even if the underlying vector field F\mathbf{F}F does not describe fluid flow; electric flux and magnetic flux - the electric and magnetic fields passing through a surface, respectively - are good examples in this vein.
