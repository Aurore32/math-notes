Gradient and the Gradient Operator

Defining the Gradient 2: Electric Boogaloo

The gradient of a scalar field ϕ(x):Rn→R\phi(\mathbf{x}): \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}ϕ(x):Rn→R is defined as the vector

∂ϕ∂xiei,\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x_i}\mathbf{e_i},∂xi​∂ϕ​ei​,

itself a vector field, where ei\mathbf{e_i}ei​ is the standard orthonormal basis in Cartesian coordinates; this we are well-aware, yet the necessity of restricting the gradient to Cartesian coordinates limits our understanding. If the gradient is the multivariable analogue of the derivative, is it possible to define the gradient independently of choice of coordinates, based simply on first principles?

Consider the definition of the derivative for uni-variate functions:

f′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)hf'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}f′(x)=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​

or, for ∣h∣<<1|h| << 1∣h∣<<1,

f(x+h)=f(x)+hf′(x)+O(h2)f(x+h) = f(x) + hf'(x) + O(h^2)f(x+h)=f(x)+hf′(x)+O(h2)

In the multi-variable case, the above holds for every single variable the function is subjected to:

f(x+h)=f(x)+h1fx1(x)+h2fx2(x)+...+hnfxn(x)+O(h2)f(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{h}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{h}_1f_{x_1}(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{h_2}f_{x_2}(\mathbf{x}) + ... + \mathbf{h_n}f_{x_n}(\mathbf{x}) + O(\mathbf{h}^2)f(x+h)=f(x)+h1​fx1​​(x)+h2​fx2​​(x)+...+hn​fxn​​(x)+O(h2)

and thus

f(x+h)=f(x)+∇f(x)⋅h+O(h2).f(\mathbf{x+h}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{h} + O(\mathbf{h}^2).f(x+h)=f(x)+∇f(x)⋅h+O(h2).

We arrive at

Definition. The coordinate-independent definition of the gradient defines the gradient ∇f(x)\nabla f(\mathbf{x})∇f(x) at a given vector-valued point x\mathbf{x}x as the vector that satisfies

f(x+h)=f(x)+∇f(x)⋅hf(\mathbf{x+h}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{h}f(x+h)=f(x)+∇f(x)⋅h

as h→0\mathbf{h} \to \mathbf{0}h→0.

The Gradient Operator

If there's anything mathematicians are great at, it's getting divorced (math comes a distant second), so it's not surprising that we proceed towards the next stage of our mathematical journey via the forceful separation of two mathematical objects that were once joined together by the decree of God. Behold:

∇\nabla∇

There is nothing more terrifying.

Definition. Define the gradient operator, denoted ∇\nabla∇ without the following function, as the quasi-vector-like object

[∂∂x1∂∂x2⋮∂∂xn]\begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix}⎣⎡​∂x1​∂​∂x2​∂​⋮∂xn​∂​​⎦⎤​

which doesn't make much sense alone, but awaits a scalar function to form the gradient ∇f\nabla f∇f defined above.

Curl and Divergence

The existence of the gradient operator, i.e. a vector-form representation of what ∇\nabla∇ does to scalar functions, allows us to generalize the gradient to vector fields F:Rn→Rn\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R^n \to R^n}F:Rn→Rn.

For a vector field, ∇F\nabla \mathbf{F}∇F - the gradient in its previously-understood meaning, i.e. a vector containing each of the partial derivatives of F\mathbf{F}F - makes little sense because each partial derivative is also a vector. What does make sense, however, is the following:

Definition. Define the divergence of a vector field F:Rn→Rn\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R^n \to R^n}F:Rn→Rn as the scalar product ∇⋅F\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}∇⋅F with the gradient operator ∇\nabla∇ as above, outputting a scalar field

div F=∇⋅F=∂Fi∂xi\text{div } \mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial x_i}div F=∇⋅F=∂xi​∂Fi​​

understood as index notation.

Besides the scalar-valued analogue obtained via the dot (scalar) product, we naturally come to the vector-valued analogue obtained via the vector (cross) product, viable only in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3:

Definition. Define the curl of a vector field F\mathbf{F}F exclusively in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 as the vector product ∇×F\nabla \times \mathbf{F}∇×F, outputting a vector field

curl F=∇×F=∣e1e2e3∂∂x1∂∂x2∂∂x3F1F2F3∣\text{curl }\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{e_1} & \mathbf{e_2} & \mathbf{e_3} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_ 1} & \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2} & \frac{\partial}{\partial x_3} \\ F_1 & F_2 & F_3 \end{vmatrix}curl F=∇×F=∣∣​e1​∂x1​∂​F1​​e2​∂x2​∂​F2​​e3​∂x3​∂​F3​​∣∣​

which, when fully expanded, equals

e1(∂F3∂x2−∂F2∂x3)+e2(∂F1∂x3−∂F3∂x1)+e3(∂F2∂x1−∂F1∂x2).\mathbf{e_1}(\frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x_3}) + \mathbf{e_2} (\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x_1}) + \mathbf{e_3} (\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x_1} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_2}).e1​(∂x2​∂F3​​−∂x3​∂F2​​)+e2​(∂x3​∂F1​​−∂x1​∂F3​​)+e3​(∂x1​∂F2​​−∂x2​∂F1​​).

Interpreting curl and divergence

Divergence. Consider a single term (non-suffix notation) within the dot product that defines divergence:

∇⋅F=∑i=1n∂Fi∂xi\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial x_i}∇⋅F=i=1∑n​∂xi​∂Fi​​

FiF_iFi​ is the component of F\mathbf{F}F along the xix_ixi​-axis, and so this term asks the question "how fast does the iiith component of F\mathbf{F}F change, or diverge, along the xix_ixi​ axis?"

Adding up the rate of change along all axes at a point gives a value that captures the total rate of change of F\mathbf{F}F at that point: whether F\mathbf{F}F is coming into, or leaving, that point.

If divergence is positive at a point x\mathbf{x}x, then the amount of F\mathbf{F}F leaving the point exceeds the amount of F\mathbf{F}F entering the point, and so we call x\mathbf{x}x a source; conversely, if divergence is negative, then F\mathbf{F}F enters the point more than it leaves, and so the point is a sink.

Curl. Curl is an immortal eldritch abomination delivered to the mortal plane from the ninth circle of Hell that defies earthly interpretation or understanding. (Also, it measures rotation.)

Basic properties

Property 1. The linear property. Grad, div and curl fulfill the properties of a linear transformation:

{∇(αf+βg)=α∇f+β∇g∇⋅(αF+βG)=α(∇⋅F)+β(∇⋅G)∇×(αF+βG)=α(∇×F)+β(∇×G)\begin{cases} \nabla (\alpha f + \beta g) = \alpha \nabla f + \beta \nabla g \\ \nabla \cdot (\alpha\mathbf{F} + \beta \mathbf{G}) = \alpha (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) + \beta (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{G}) \\ \nabla \times (\alpha \mathbf{F} + \beta \mathbf{G}) = \alpha (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) + \beta (\nabla \times \mathbf{G}) \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​∇(αf+βg)=α∇f+β∇g∇⋅(αF+βG)=α(∇⋅F)+β(∇⋅G)∇×(αF+βG)=α(∇×F)+β(∇×G)​

for scalar fields fff and ggg, vector fields F\mathbf{F}F and G\mathbf{G}G, and constants α\alphaα and β\betaβ.

Property 2. The Leibniz property. Each of grad, div and curl fulfill some generalization of the product rule:

{∇(fg)=(∇f)g+f(∇g)∇⋅(Fg)=(∇⋅F)g+F⋅(∇g)∇×(Fg)=(∇×F)g+(∇g)×F\begin{cases} \nabla(fg) = (\nabla f) g + f(\nabla g) \\ \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{F}g) = (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})g + \mathbf{F} \cdot (\nabla g) \\ \nabla \times (\mathbf{F}g) = (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})g + (\nabla g)\times \mathbf{F} \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​∇(fg)=(∇f)g+f(∇g)∇⋅(Fg)=(∇⋅F)g+F⋅(∇g)∇×(Fg)=(∇×F)g+(∇g)×F​

noting that the last term is (∇g)×F(\nabla g) \times \mathbf{F}(∇g)×F, rather than the other way around.

Further properties follow from dot-product and cross-product properties of vectors, which ∇\nabla∇ counts itself among.

Irrotational and solenoidal fields

Irrotational fields

Definition. If we accept unambiguously and wholly without justification that the curl of a vector field F\mathbf{F}F, ∇×F\nabla \times \mathbf{F}∇×F, quantifies its rotation at a point, then we find ourselves obliged to define an irrotational field - a field which has no rotation - as that which satisfies

∇×F=0\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0∇×F=0

at all points.

The following theorem provides a connection between irrotational fields and conservative fields:

Theorem. If F\mathbf{F}F is a vector field defined everywhere on R3\mathbb{R^3}R3, then irrotational implies conservatism:

∇×F=0  ⟺  F=∇ϕ\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0 \iff \mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi∇×F=0⟺F=∇ϕ

for some scalar field ϕ\phiϕ.

Ronald Reagan would be spinning in his grave, but he can't, because conservative implies irrotational.

Proof.

If F=∇ϕ\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phiF=∇ϕ, then we have

∇×F=∇×(∇ϕ)=∇×(∂ϕ∂xi)=ϵijk∂∂xj∂∂xkϕ=0\begin{aligned} \nabla \times \mathbf{F} &= \nabla \times (\nabla \phi) \\ &= \nabla \times (\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x_i}) \\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\frac{\partial}{\partial x_k} \phi \\ &= 0 \end{aligned}∇×F​=∇×(∇ϕ)=∇×(∂xi​∂ϕ​)=ϵijk​∂xj​∂​∂xk​∂​ϕ=0​

arguing that for every permutation ϵijk\epsilon_{ijk}ϵijk​ and its corresponding ϵikj\epsilon_{ikj}ϵikj​, the mixed second derivatives

∂2ϕ∂xj∂xk=∂2ϕ∂xk∂xj\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial x_j \partial x_k} = \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x_k \partial x_j}∂xj​∂xk​∂2ϕ​=∂xk​∂xj​∂2ϕ​

are equal, but ϵijk=−ϵikj\epsilon_{ijk} = -\epsilon_{ikj}ϵijk​=−ϵikj​, forming a pair that sums to zero.

Conversely, we find the nearest corner, curl up into fetal position, and cry for the next half-hour. (Or at least, until le Grande Théorème Intégral swoops in and saves us - but that'll be much later!)

Corollary. The above theorem implies that the curl of conservative fields is zero:

∇×(∇ϕ)=0\nabla\times (\nabla \phi) = 0∇×(∇ϕ)=0

for some scalar field ϕ\phiϕ.

Solenoidal fields

Definition. Define a solenoidal or a divergence-free field as a vector field F\mathbf{F}F which satisfies ∇⋅F=0\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0∇⋅F=0 at every point where it is defined.

Solenoidal fields derive their names from solenoids: coils of wires that produce magnetic fields B\mathbf{B}B which are divergence-free, seen below.

No point in the field can be understood as a source or sink; the field leaves and enters each point in equal amounts. Solenoidal fields connect themselves to the concept of curl through the following theorem:

Theorem. Given a solenoidal field F\mathbf{F}F satisfying ∇⋅F=0\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0∇⋅F=0, we have

F=∇×A\mathbf{F = \nabla \times A}F=∇×A

for some vector field A\mathbf{A}A, provided that F\mathbf{F}F is defined everywhere on R3\mathbb{R^3}R3.

Proof.

Again, the forward direction is fairly straightforward: if F=∇×A\mathbf{F = \nabla \times A}F=∇×A, then we have

∇⋅F=∇⋅(∇×A)=∇⋅(ϵijk∂∂xjAk)=∂∂xi(ϵijk∂∂xjAk)\begin{aligned} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} &= \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \\ &= \nabla \cdot (\epsilon_{ijk}\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}A_k) \\ &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(\epsilon_{ijk}\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}A_k) \end{aligned}∇⋅F​=∇⋅(∇×A)=∇⋅(ϵijk​∂xj​∂​Ak​)=∂xi​∂​(ϵijk​∂xj​∂​Ak​)​

which vanishes due to symmetry.

Conversely, we now are no longer reduced to crying and vomiting; instead, for an arbitrary point in the plane (x0,y0,z0)(\mathbf{x_0,y_0,z_0})(x0​,y0​,z0​), we claim an actual solution for F=∇×A\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}F=∇×A in the form

A(x)=(∫z0zFy(x,y,z′) dz′,∫x0xFz(x′,y,z0) dx′−∫z0zFx(x,y,z′) dz′, 0)\mathbf{A(x)} = (\int_{z_0}^z F_y(x,y,z')\ dz', \int_{x_0}^x F_z(x',y,z_0)\ dx' - \int_{z_0}^z F_x(x,y,z')\ dz',\ 0)A(x)=(∫z0​z​Fy​(x,y,z′) dz′,∫x0​x​Fz​(x′,y,z0​) dx′−∫z0​z​Fx​(x,y,z′) dz′, 0)
