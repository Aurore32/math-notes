Stokes' Theorem

The big ideas

Generalizing Green's Theorem

Recall from the previous section that Green's theorem related microscopic circulation - the infinitesimal rotation of a vector field at every point in a region - to macroscopic circulation, the total rotation of that vector field around the boundary of the region.

The big horse is the unholy amalgamation of all of the smaller horses.

Stokes' theorem generalizes such a concept to any region and any boundary surface (with one less dimension than the region) in any-dimensional space, no matter how curved or straight, or in polar or Cartesian coordinates.

An infinite number of infinitesimal horses are spinning in place in George Green's pasture, enclosed by a fence. Mr. Green asked the horses politely if they could move out of the pasture, so that he could go harvest some potatoes and carrots; the horses neighed, and POOF! One giant horse was all that was left behind, now tip-toeing its dainty feet around the top of the fence.

This is where all the first-year undergraduate math students in the room erupted in raucous laughter, for they had just studied Green's theorem and wished to demonstrate their familiarity with the concept. "But wait," one particularly well-studied student chimed in. "What if the pasture wasn't flat at all - what if the horses were grazing on the side of a mountain, and not in the xyxyxy Cartesian plane?"

Of course, that student did not know that Green's theorem was applicable to non-Cartesian coordinate systems; but to explain that would be putting Descartes before the horse.

(Please clap.)

The statement goes:

Theorem. Stokes' Theorem. For SSS a smooth surface in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 and C=∂SC = \partial SC=∂S its boundary curve, Stokes' theorem states for a smooth vector field F(x)\mathbf{F(x)}F(x) that

∫S(∇×F)⋅dS=∫CF⋅dx,\int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{x},∫S​(∇×F)⋅dS=∫C​F⋅dx,

where dx=(dx,dy,dz)d\mathbf{x} = (dx,dy,dz)dx=(dx,dy,dz), and the orientations of SSS and CCC are assumed to be compatible, meaning that SSS doesn't bat for the other team the chosen directions of the tangent to CCC, t\mathbf{t}t, and the normal to SSS, n\mathbf{n}n, have their cross product t×n\mathbf{t} \times \mathbf{n}t×n pointing out of the surface.

In particular, this is satisfied if n\mathbf{n}n is chosen to point out of the surface and t\mathbf{t}t is chosen to be in the anticlockwise direction (pictured above).

A new understanding of curl

Let's elaborate a little bit on Green's theorem again. Previously, we inferred - but by no means rigorously justified - the statement that the left-hand side of Green's theorem, featuring the integrand

∂Q∂x−∂P∂y,\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​,

equalling the divergence of the normal vector to F\mathbf{F}F, was a measure of the rotation of the field - and thus a two-dimensional analogue to curl.

Stokes' theorem allows us to codify this notion a bit more clearly: the integrand of Stokes' theorem features curl, with

∫S(∇×F)⋅dS=∫CF⋅dx;\int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{x};∫S​(∇×F)⋅dS=∫C​F⋅dx;

As with the Divergence Theorem and its recontextualization of divergence, we may now consider an infinitesimal surface SSS shrunken to contain only the single point in space x\mathbf{x}x. In this infinitesimal surface SxS_xSx​, the curl is constant, and so the left-hand side surface integral is simply the surface area SSS times the constant:

∫Sx(∇×F)⋅dS=Sn⋅(∇×F)\int_{S_x} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot \mathbf{dS} = S\mathbf{n}\cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})∫Sx​​(∇×F)⋅dS=Sn⋅(∇×F)

where n\mathbf{n}n is the normal to the surface.

As such, we define

Definition. Coordinate-free definition of curl. For a vector field F\mathbf{F}F and a normal vector n\mathbf{n}n to an infinitesimal surface SSS with area ∣S∣|S|∣S∣, define the curl of F\mathbf{F}F as the vector-valued function given by the limit

n⋅(∇×F)=lim⁡S→01∣S∣∫∂SF⋅dx.\mathbf{n} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = \lim_{S \to 0}\frac{1}{|S|}\int_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot \mathbf{dx}.n⋅(∇×F)=S→0lim​∣S∣1​∫∂S​F⋅dx.

We can interpret this as the curl ∇×F\nabla \times \mathbf{F}∇×F representing a circulation density (circulation in an infinitesimal point ∫∂SF⋅dx\int_{\partial S}\mathbf{F\cdot dx}∫∂S​F⋅dx, divided by the area of that point), and the dot product n⋅(∇×F)\mathbf{n}\cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})n⋅(∇×F) representing the circulation density in a particular plane normal to n\mathbf{n}n.

This definition makes the intuition behind Stokes' theorem a little bit easier to understand, notwithstanding the fact that the definition is only true if Stokes' theorem is true in the first place.

The curl ∇×F\nabla \times \mathbf{F}∇×F represents the local circulation at every point in a region; for two neighboring points, a clockwise circulation for one is an anticlockwise circulation for the other, and so the circulations cancel and cancel when integrated throughout the region until only the circulation on the very edge remains.

Sketching a proof

Proof.

Stokes' Theorem is Green's Theorem across a general surface, rather than a surface lying in the xyxyxy-plane. Let SSS be a smooth surface in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 parameterizable by S=x(u,v)S = \mathbf{x}(u,v)S=x(u,v); as such, our (non-Cartesian) coordinate plane of operations is the uvuvuv-plane rather than the xyxyxy-plane. Now all that remains is proving that Stokes' theorem is indeed valid for the uvuvuv-plane.

Corollary: irrotational fields are conservative fields

Proof.

An irrotational field F\mathbf{F}F, by definition, satisfies ∇×F=0\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0∇×F=0. By Stokes' theorem, we have

∫S(∇×F)⋅dS=∫S0 dS=0\int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_S 0\ dS = 0∫S​(∇×F)⋅dS=∫S​0 dS=0

∮CF⋅dx\oint_C \mathbf{F \cdot dx}∮C​F⋅dx

for any surface SSS, and thus any closed curve CCC in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3.
