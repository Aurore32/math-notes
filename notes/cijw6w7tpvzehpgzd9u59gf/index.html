Definitions and Properties

Definition (Vector space). A vector space VVV over R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C is a collection of entities ("vectors") v∈V\mathbf{v} \in Vv∈V, in which we define two operations: addition of two vectors and scalar multiplication with a vector.

These "vectors" (notice that I am stubbornly using quotation marks here like a conspiracy theorist who believes that Big Archimedes fabricated vectors to steal your money, because technically, we aren't supposed to know what vectors are yet; we're defining them here) must satisfy the following properties under addition and scalar multiplication:

Addition

  1. Commutativity: a+b=b+a\mathbf{a + b = b + a}a+b=b+a
  2. Associativity (order does not change result): (a+b)+c=a+(b+c)\mathbf{(a+b)+c=a+(b+c)}(a+b)+c=a+(b+c)
  3. Identity: there is a vector 0\mathbf{0}0 such that a+0=a\mathbf{a+0=a}a+0=a.
  4. Existence of inverses: for all vectors a\mathbf{a}a, there exists another vector in VVV which we denote −a-\mathbf{a}−a such that a+(−a)=0\mathbf{a+(-a)=0}a+(−a)=0.

  4. Identity: 1a=a1\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{a}1a=a.

It is implicitly stated that the sum of two "vectors" in VVV (suspicious name, that) will be another "vector" (also in VVV), and the scalar multiplication of a vector will give another vector; thus we can say that vector spaces are closed under addition and scalar multiplication; that is, these two operations map elements of VVV to VVV.

Notice also that vectors (as we understand them; the matrix kind) of any size will all satisfy these properties, whether they have two or three or ten entries; in fact, vectors with a single entry - which are just scalars - also satisfy these properties. Thus, any Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn - the set of nnn-tuples of real numbers - are vector spaces; all lines through the origins are vector spaces (as in, all points on these lines), but lines not through the origin are not - they do not contain (0,0), the zero vector.

The geometric meaning of vectors, of course, are more familiar to us: objects v\mathbf{v}v with a length denoted ∣v∣|\mathbf{v}|∣v∣, and a direction which remains unchanged under scalar multiplication (either parallel or antiparallel).

Definition (Unit vector). A unit vector, usually denoted with a little hat like this - v^\mathbf{\hat{v}}v^ - is a vector with length 1.
