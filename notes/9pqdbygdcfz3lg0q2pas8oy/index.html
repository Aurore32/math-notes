Solving the Laplace Equation

Properties of the Laplace equation

Definition. The Laplace equation is defined as ∇2Φ=0\nabla^2\Phi = 0∇2Φ=0 for a scalar field Φ\PhiΦ, where ∇2\nabla^2∇2 is the operator known as the Laplacian.

Time for a few quick properties of this partial differential equation which should help us out a tiny bit!

Property 1. Linearity. If Φ\PhiΦ and Ψ\PsiΨ are both solutions to the Laplace equation, then any linear combination of the two λΦ+μΨ\lambda \Phi + \mu \PsiλΦ+μΨ for scalars λ,μ\lambda, \muλ,μ also solves the Laplace equation.

This is swiftly verified via the linearity of the Laplacian operator itself.

Expressed in differential form, the Laplace equation ppears as follows:

for a scalar field Φ\PhiΦ in Rn\mathbb{R^n}Rn.

It's natural to broadly conceptualize solutions to Φ\PhiΦ into one of either two types: types where the solution is symmetric - ∂2Φ∂xi2\frac{\partial^2 \Phi}{\partial x_i^2}∂xi2​∂2Φ​ is the same in every single direction xix_ixi​, and thus equals zero - and types where the solution is not, and the function has different second partial derivatives along the axes and symmetry is lost.

This leads us to the following property:

Radial symmetry and isotropic solutions

Property 2. Radial symmetry. If Φ\PhiΦ is solely a function of the radius rrr from the origin, e.g. in polar or spherical coordinates - Φ=Φ(r)\Phi = \Phi(r)Φ=Φ(r) - then Φ\PhiΦ is symmetrical about all the coordinate axes; thus, if we can guarantee that Φ\PhiΦ has second derivative zero about one of the axes, it satisfies the Laplace equation by default.

Definition. Call such a solution Φ=Φ(r)\Phi = \Phi(r)Φ=Φ(r) to ∇2Φ=0\nabla^2 \Phi = 0∇2Φ=0 isotropic: it has no preferred direction.

If we were to construct such directional-derivative-bound solutions, our basic building block would be:

Definition. Denote the isotropic solution to Laplace's equation in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 as the fundamental solution, or the unit monopole, if it has the property that the flux of its gradient out of a sphere of any radius is equal to one.

The flux of the gradient ∇Φ\nabla \Phi∇Φ has the convenient physical interpretation of being either gravitational flux (for Φ\PhiΦ equalling gravitational potential, where ∇Φ\nabla \Phi∇Φ is gravitational force) or electric flux.

The fundamental solution is also known as the unit monopole because it's a hypothetical representation of a single magnetic pole (north or south), existing independent of its counterpart and freely suspended in space; despite every mathematical indication towards their existence and despite scientists' best attempts at smashing three billion dollars' worth of funding to bits and pieces in the Large Hadron Collider, monopoles have never been found outside of physicists' wet dreams and in-between the arms of your mom at the epileptic strip club down the street.
