Phase-Portrait Analysis

Recall that our solution to the linear system of differential equations

x˙=Ax\mathbf{\dot{x}} = \mathbf{Ax}x˙=Ax

x=c1eλ1xv1+c2eλ2xv2+⋯+cneλnxvn.\mathbf{x}=c_1e^{\lambda_1 x }v_1 + c_2e^{\lambda_2 x}v_2 + \dots + c_ne^{\lambda_n x}v_n.x=c1​eλ1​xv1​+c2​eλ2​xv2​+⋯+cn​eλn​xvn​.

Let us write this in the form

x=y1v1+y2v2+…ynvn.\mathbf{x} =y_1v_1 + y_2 v_2 + \dots y_n v_n.x=y1​v1​+y2​v2​+…yn​vn​.

Eigenvectors are extremely valuable to us because they are essentially the "axes" upon which our solution x\mathbf{x}x acts on - if we vary only a single variable yky_kyk​, then we know that the solution has only changed along the eigenvector vkv_kvk​. This allows us to build up a "phase-portrait" of our solution x\mathbf{x}x: a graph of how the vector x\mathbf{x}x changes as y1,y2,…,yny_1, y_2, \dots, y_ny1​,y2​,…,yn​ vary, taking these as our axes.

For our sanity's sake, let's consider only the two-dimensional case (with y1y_1y1​ and y2y_2y2​ as axes) and analyze the differing examples that arise out of the different forms of y1y_1y1​ and y2y_2y2​.

Spiral points

If λ1\lambda_1λ1​, λ2\lambda_2λ2​ are complex conjugates (they must be, given that they are the solutions to a quadratic equation), we have three different cases. Let λ1,2=a±bi\lambda_{1,2} = a \pm biλ1,2​=a±bi, resulting in y1,2=eax(cos⁡bx±sin⁡bx)y_{1,2} = e^{ax}(\cos{bx}\pm\sin{bx})y1,2​=eax(cosbx±sinbx). Then we have a phase portrait that looks like a spiral:

Why a spiral? Because if we consider the cos⁡bx+sin⁡bx\cos bx + \sin bxcosbx+sinbx term on its own, that multiplied by a vector gives us an ellipse (as we know ellipses have parametric equations x=acos⁡t,y=bsin⁡tx=a\cos t, y=b\sin tx=acost,y=bsint). But when the eaxe^{ax}eax term comes in, this "ellipse" either expands exponentially, creating an outward spiral, or collapses inward exponentially, creating an inward spiral. Alternatively, if the real part is zero, the phase portrait is simply two ellipses centered at the origin.
