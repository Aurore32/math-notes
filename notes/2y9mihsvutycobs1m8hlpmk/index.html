Orthogonal Curvilinear Coordinates

On the oxymoronic qualities of curvilinearity

Curvilinear is a seriously funny word; in fact, it might just be out-competing "thank God I'm an atheist", "fun fact", and "happy mathematician" as my favorite oxymoron of all time. Thankfully, though, orthogonal curvilinear coordinates do not satisfy the two conditions

  1. It is curved, and
  2. It is linear;

Instead, they satisfy the conditions detailed below.

Definition. Orthogonal curvilinear coordinates are any coordinate system x=(x1,x2,x3)\mathbf{x} = (x_1,x_2,x_3)x=(x1​,x2​,x3​) in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3 where the tangent vectors to x\mathbf{x}x along the x1x_1x1​-, x2x_2x2​-, and x3x_3x3​-axes form a right-handed orthonormal basis:

∂x∂xi⋅∂x∂xj=δij, (∂x∂xj)×(∂x∂xk)=ϵijk∂x∂xi\frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial x_i}\cdot \frac{\partial\mathbf{x}}{\partial x_j} = \delta_{ij},\ (\frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial x_j})\times (\frac{\partial\mathbf{x}}{\partial x_k}) = \epsilon_{ijk}\frac{\partial\mathbf{x}}{\partial x_i}∂xi​∂x​⋅∂xj​∂x​=δij​, (∂xj​∂x​)×(∂xk​∂x​)=ϵijk​∂xi​∂x​

e.g. ∂x2x×∂x3x=∂x1x\partial_{x_2}\mathbf{x}\times\partial_{x_3}\mathbf{x} = \partial_{x_1}\mathbf{x}∂x2​​x×∂x3​​x=∂x1​​x.

Let's pick apart this definition one bit at a time.

First, for a coordinate system - say the standard Cartesian coordinate system x=(x,y,z)\mathbf{x} = (x,y,z)x=(x,y,z) - what do the tangent vectors ∂xx,∂yx,∂zx\partial_x\mathbf{x}, \partial_y\mathbf{x}, \partial_z\mathbf{x}∂x​x,∂y​x,∂z​x indicate? ∂xx\partial_x\mathbf{x}∂x​x, for example, denotes the change of x\mathbf{x}x as we move a small step along the xxx-axis; in other words, it indicates the direction the xxx-axis is moving at the point x\mathbf{x}x - more commonly known as the basis vector for the xxx-axis, ex\mathbf{e}_xex​.

This clues us in a little bit: we can say much more generally that for any coordinate system (x1,x2,x3)(x_1, x_2, x_3)(x1​,x2​,x3​), the tangent vectors ∂x1x,∂x2x,∂x3x\partial_{x_1} \mathbf{x}, \partial_{x_2}\mathbf{x}, \partial_{x_3}\mathbf{x}∂x1​​x,∂x2​​x,∂x3​​x - upon normalization, as convention dictates - points us in the direction of the basis vectors e1,e2,e3\mathbf{e_1, e_2, e_3}e1​,e2​,e3​ of the coordinate system at that point. In particular, we call

hx1=∣∂x1x∣, hx2=∣∂x2x∣, hx3=∣∂x3x∣h_{x_1} = |\partial_{x_1} \mathbf{x}|,\ h_{x_2} = |\partial_{x_2} \mathbf{x}|,\ h_{x_3} = |\partial_{x_3} \mathbf{x}|hx1​​=∣∂x1​​x∣, hx2​​=∣∂x2​​x∣, hx3​​=∣∂x3​​x∣

scale factors that tell us the change in length along each coordinate axis.

An orthogonal curvilinear coordinate system is thus one where the basis vectors form a right-handed perpendicular system (orthogonal), but not necessarily constant at every point (curvilinear).

Cylindrical and spherical polar coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates

Take the cylindrical coordinate system in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 as an example of orthogonal curvilinear coordinates:

x=(x,y,z)=(rcos⁡θ,rsin⁡θ,z)\mathbf{x} = (x,y,z) = (r\cos \theta, r\sin \theta, z)x=(x,y,z)=(rcosθ,rsinθ,z)

resulting in the tangent vectors

{∂rx=(cos⁡θ,sin⁡θ,0)∂θx=(−rsin⁡θ,rcos⁡θ,0)=(−sin⁡θ,cos⁡θ,0) (normalized)∂zx=(0,0,1)\begin{cases} \partial_r\mathbf{x} = (\cos \theta, \sin \theta, 0) \\ \partial_\theta \mathbf{x} = (-r\sin \theta, r\cos\theta, 0) = (-\sin \theta, \cos \theta, 0) \text{ (normalized)} \\ \partial_z \mathbf{x} = (0,0,1) \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​∂r​x=(cosθ,sinθ,0)∂θ​x=(−rsinθ,rcosθ,0)=(−sinθ,cosθ,0) (normalized)∂z​x=(0,0,1)​

with associated scale factors 1,r,11, r, 11,r,1 which are mutually orthogonal, but not at all constant.

Spherical coordinates

For the coordinate system (r,θ,ϕ)(r, \theta, \phi)(r,θ,ϕ), we have

x=(x,y,z)=(rsin⁡θcos⁡ϕ,rsin⁡θsin⁡ϕ,rcos⁡θ)\mathbf{x} = (x,y,z) = (r\sin\theta\cos\phi, r\sin\theta\sin\phi, r\cos\theta)x=(x,y,z)=(rsinθcosϕ,rsinθsinϕ,rcosθ)

and thus the tangent vectors and scale factors

{∂rx=(sin⁡θcos⁡ϕ,sin⁡θsin⁡ϕ,cos⁡θ),hr=1∂θx=(rcos⁡θcos⁡ϕ,rcos⁡θsin⁡ϕ,−rsin⁡θ),hθ=r∂ϕx=(−rsin⁡θsin⁡ϕ,rsin⁡θcos⁡ϕ,0),hϕ=rsin⁡θ\begin{cases} \partial_r \mathbf{x} = (\sin \theta \cos \phi, \sin \theta \sin \phi, \cos \theta), h_r = 1 \\ \partial_\theta \mathbf{x} = (r\cos\theta\cos\phi, r\cos\theta\sin\phi, -r\sin\theta), h_\theta = r \\ \partial_\phi \mathbf{x} = (-r\sin\theta\sin\phi, r\sin\theta\cos\phi, 0), h_\phi = r\sin\theta \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​∂r​x=(sinθcosϕ,sinθsinϕ,cosθ),hr​=1∂θ​x=(rcosθcosϕ,rcosθsinϕ,−rsinθ),hθ​=r∂ϕ​x=(−rsinθsinϕ,rsinθcosϕ,0),hϕ​=rsinθ​

Differential operators in curvilinear coordinates

The gradient and the gradient operator

Recall our previous coordinate-independent definition of the gradient ∇f\nabla f∇f of a scalar field f(x)f(\mathbf{x})f(x) as being the vector satisfying

df=∇f⋅dxdf = \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{x}df=∇f⋅dx

for dx→0d\mathbf{x} \to 0dx→0, where dxd\mathbf{x}dx is the vector given by

dx=(∂x1x) dx1+(∂x2x) dx2+(∂x3x) dx3d\mathbf{x} = (\partial_{x_1}\mathbf{x})\ dx_1 + (\partial_{x_2}\mathbf{x})\ dx_2 + (\partial_{x_3}\mathbf{x})\ dx_3dx=(∂x1​​x) dx1​+(∂x2​​x) dx2​+(∂x3​​x) dx3​

i.e. the total change in position resulting from a small change along each of the coordinate axes.

This definition is valid for any coordinate system (x1,x2,x3)(x_1,x_2,x_3)(x1​,x2​,x3​), with orthogonal curvilinear basis (e1,e2,e3)(\mathbf{e_1, e_2, e_3})(e1​,e2​,e3​). For such a coordinate system, we also have

df=(∂x1f) dx1+(∂x2f) dx2+(∂x3f) dx3df = (\partial_{x_1} f)\ dx_1 + (\partial_{x_2} f)\ dx_2 + (\partial_{x_3} f)\ dx_3df=(∂x1​​f) dx1​+(∂x2​​f) dx2​+(∂x3​​f) dx3​

with a comparison of coefficients yielding

(∂x1f) dx1+(∂x2f) dx2+(∂x3f) dx3=∇f⋅((∂x1x) dx1+(∂x2x) dx2+(∂x3x) dx3)=∇f⋅(h1e1dx1+h2e2dx2+h3e3dx3)\begin{aligned} (\partial_{x_1} f)\ dx_1 + (\partial_{x_2} f)\ dx_2 + (\partial_{x_3} f)\ dx_3 &= \nabla f \cdot ((\partial_{x_1}\mathbf{x})\ dx_1 + (\partial_{x_2}\mathbf{x})\ dx_2 + (\partial_{x_3}\mathbf{x})\ dx_3) \\ &= \nabla f \cdot (h_1 \mathbf{e_1}dx_1 + h_2\mathbf{e_2}dx_2 + h_3\mathbf{e_3}dx_3) \end{aligned}(∂x1​​f) dx1​+(∂x2​​f) dx2​+(∂x3​​f) dx3​​=∇f⋅((∂x1​​x) dx1​+(∂x2​​x) dx2​+(∂x3​​x) dx3​)=∇f⋅(h1​e1​dx1​+h2​e2​dx2​+h3​e3​dx3​)​

combining all the above. Suppose now that the gradient vector ∇f\nabla f∇f can be written in the orthogonal curvilinear basis (e1,e2,e3)(\mathbf{e_1, e_2, e_3})(e1​,e2​,e3​) as

∇f=α1e1+α2e2+α3e3\nabla f = \alpha_1 \mathbf{e_1} + \alpha_2 \mathbf{e_2} + \alpha_3 \mathbf{e_3}∇f=α1​e1​+α2​e2​+α3​e3​

By the orthonormality of this basis, we obtain the expression

(∂x1f) dx1+(∂x2f) dx2+(∂x3f) dx3=∇f⋅(h1e1dx1+h2e2dx2+h3e3dx3)=(α1e1+α2e2+α3e3)⋅(h1e1dx1+h2e2dx2+h3e3dx3)=α1h1dx1+α2h2dx2+α3h3dx3                                             as ei⋅ei=1\begin{aligned} &(\partial_{x_1} f)\ dx_1 + (\partial_{x_2} f)\ dx_2 + (\partial_{x_3} f)\ dx_3 = \nabla f \cdot (h_1 \mathbf{e_1}dx_1 + h_2\mathbf{e_2}dx_2 + h_3\mathbf{e_3}dx_3) \\ &= (\alpha_1 \mathbf{e_1} + \alpha_2 \mathbf{e_2} + \alpha_3 \mathbf{e_3})\cdot (h_1 \mathbf{e_1}dx_1 + h_2\mathbf{e_2}dx_2 + h_3\mathbf{e_3}dx_3) \\ &= \alpha_1 h_1 dx_1 + \alpha_2 h_2 dx_2 + \alpha_3 h_3 dx_3 \text{ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ as $\mathbf{e_i\cdot e_i} = 1$} \\ \end{aligned}​(∂x1​​f) dx1​+(∂x2​​f) dx2​+(∂x3​​f) dx3​=∇f⋅(h1​e1​dx1​+h2​e2​dx2​+h3​e3​dx3​)=(α1​e1​+α2​e2​+α3​e3​)⋅(h1​e1​dx1​+h2​e2​dx2​+h3​e3​dx3​)=α1​h1​dx1​+α2​h2​dx2​+α3​h3​dx3​                                             as ei​⋅ei​=1​

Comparing coefficients of dx1,dx2dx_1, dx_2dx1​,dx2​ and dx3dx_3dx3​ now yield

{α1=1h1(∂x1f)α2=1h2(∂x2f)α3=1h3(∂x3f)\begin{cases} \alpha_1 = \frac{1}{h_1}(\partial_{x_1} f) \\ \alpha_2 = \frac{1}{h_2}(\partial_{x_2} f) \\ \alpha_3 = \frac{1}{h_3}(\partial_{x_3} f) \\ \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​α1​=h1​1​(∂x1​​f)α2​=h2​1​(∂x2​​f)α3​=h3​1​(∂x3​​f)​

and thus

Theorem. The gradient in general orthogonal curvilinear coordinates (x1,x2,x3)(x_1,x_2,x_3)(x1​,x2​,x3​).

∇f=1h1(∂x1f)e1+1h2(∂x2f)e2+1h3(∂x3f)e3\nabla f = \frac{1}{h_1}(\partial_{x_1} f)\mathbf{e_1} + \frac{1}{h_2}(\partial_{x_2} f)\mathbf{e_2} + \frac{1}{h_3}(\partial_{x_3} f)\mathbf{e_3}∇f=h1​1​(∂x1​​f)e1​+h2​1​(∂x2​​f)e2​+h3​1​(∂x3​​f)e3​

with the newfound scale-factor terms in the denominator intuitively understood as "scaling down" the rates of change in the x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​ and x3x_3x3​ axes (for instance, a single step in the x1x_1x1​ axis is worth h1h_1h1​ length-units instead of 111 length-unit, so ∂x1f\partial_{x_1}f∂x1​​f is h1h_1h1​ times longer than it should be).

Associated with this is

Theorem. The gradient operator in curvilinear coordinates, derived from above as

∇=e1h1∂x1+e2h2∂x2+e3h3∂x3.\nabla = \frac{\mathbf{e_1}}{h_1} \partial_{x_1} + \frac{\mathbf{e_2}}{h_2} \partial_{x_2} + \frac{\mathbf{e_3}}{h_3} \partial_{x_3}.∇=h1​e1​​∂x1​​+h2​e2​​∂x2​​+h3​e3​​∂x3​​.

The abstracted gradient operator can then be used to derive expressions for divergence and curl in different coordinate systems; unfortunately, this stone shall be left unturned until we introduce the Big Integral Theorems.
