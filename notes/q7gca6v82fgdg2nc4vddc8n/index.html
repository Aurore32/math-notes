Left regular action

Some group actions act on the group themselves: that is to say, a function ϕ\phiϕ can be defined that takes every element g∈Gg\in Gg∈G to a permutation of the elements of GGG. The simplest such action is the

Definition. Left regular action. For a group GGG, define the left regular action of GGG on itself via the function ϕ(g)(x)=g⋅Gx\phi(g)(x) = g\cdot_G xϕ(g)(x)=g⋅G​x: the action of ggg on xxx is just ggg multiplied by xxx. This action is faithful and transitive.

The fact that this is an action at all derives from the group axioms; its faithfulness derives from gx=xgx = xgx=x implying necessarily that g=eg = eg=e, by the uniqueness of the identity; and its transitiveness derives from ϕ(yx−1)(x)=y\phi(yx^{-1})(x) = yϕ(yx−1)(x)=y for any x,y∈Gx, y\in Gx,y∈G.

Through the framework of a regular action, we are now equipped to prove

Theorem. Cayley's Theorem. Every single group is isomorphic to a subgroup of some symmetric group.

Proof.

Via the left regular action, a homomorphism ϕ:G→Sym(G)\phi: G \to \text{Sym}(G)ϕ:G→Sym(G) can be defined whose kernel is {e}\{e\}{e}; by the FIRST ISOMORPHISM THEOREM!!!, we then have

G/Ker(ϕ)=G/{e}=G≅Im(ϕ)≤Sym(G).G/\text{Ker}(\phi) = G/\{e\} = G \cong \text{Im}(\phi) \leq \text{Sym}(G).G/Ker(ϕ)=G/{e}=G≅Im(ϕ)≤Sym(G).

On the surface, Cayley's theorem seems like a surprising result; symmetric groups just describe ways of swapping the order of things around, after all - so how can groups of all kinds, from quaternions to the integers to a dihedral group acting on polygons, be encapsulated in nothing more than those permutations? Once you think about it, though, it's not really that weird! Let's say g∈Gg \in Gg∈G is a product of two elements g1g2g_1 g_2g1​g2​:

g=g1g2g = g_1 g_2g=g1​g2​

All that Cayley's theorem says is that multiplication by g1g_1g1​ can be thought of as the permutation that makes g2g_2g2​ swap places with ggg - something which holds true for all combinations of elements in GGG, as per the left regular action.

The conjugation action

Definition. Let a,ba, ba,b both be elements of a group GGG. Define the conjugation of aaa by bbb as the group element given by c=bab−1∈Gc= bab^{-1} \in Gc=bab−1∈G; conversely, if there exists some b∈Gb \in Gb∈G such that c=bab−1c = bab^{-1}c=bab−1, call aaa and ccc conjugates.

What is the point of conjugation? If we interpret multiplication by an element in a group as "doing something" - e.g. multiplication by a permutation is switching the order of a set, and multiplication by a cyclic group element is a rotation - then the expression bab−1bab^{-1}bab−1 just means "doing bbb first, then aaa, then reversing bbb". In non-abelian groups, this is markedly different from just doing aaa; we are placing aaa in the context of having done bbb first.

(To get a sense for why these two things might be different, compare "undressing in your own home" vs. "driving to the closest kindergarten in your neighborhood, undressing once you've gotten there, and driving back home." Though I suspect you probably won't be getting to the "driving back home" part if you do this.)

But that doesn't mean the conjugates bab−1bab^{-1}bab−1 and aaa are completely different; we've placed the action aaa in a different context, but it's still the action aaa. For that reason, several similarities arise; for instance, the conjugate of an element has the same order as the element itself, with

(bab−1)k=bakb−1=e  ⟺  ak=e.(bab^{-1})^k = ba^k b^{-1} = e \iff a^k = e.(bab−1)k=bakb−1=e⟺ak=e.
