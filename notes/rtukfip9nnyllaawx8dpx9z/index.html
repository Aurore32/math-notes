Generalizing the area integral

We know the drill. Just as a single integral is formed over a single Riemann sum, and an area (double) integral is formed over a double Riemann sum, a volume integral - just to remind you, we're now at three - is formed over a triple Riemann sum, based off the volume differential

dV=dx dy dz.dV = dx\ dy\ dz.dV=dx dy dz.

Change of variables for volume integrals

Suppose now that (x,y,z)→(u(x,y,z),v(x,y,z),w(x,y,z))(x,y,z) \to (u(x,y,z),v(x,y,z),w(x,y,z))(x,y,z)→(u(x,y,z),v(x,y,z),w(x,y,z)) is a change of variables from x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z to u,v,wu, v, wu,v,w, where all of u,v,wu, v, wu,v,w are smooth, invertible functions of x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z; in particular, denote their inverses as

{x=x(u,v,w)y=y(u,v,w)z=z(u,v,w)\begin{cases} x = x(u,v,w) \\ y = y(u,v,w) \\ z = z(u,v,w) \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​x=x(u,v,w)y=y(u,v,w)z=z(u,v,w)​

Wrapping up

Three last concerns before we wave sayonara and leave the world of multiple integrals behind forever. That is, until next Monday.

  1. Finding volume with volume integrals (incredible). Our puny three-dimensionaloid minds know not how to interpret ∫Dϕ dV\int_D \phi \ dV∫D​ϕ dV as volume or area under a curve, but ∫D dV\int_D \ dV∫D​ dV represents the sum of the infinitesimal cubes making up the solid DDD and thus converges to its volume.

  2. Generalizing volume integrals to higher dimensions. Three points of concern here:

    • By Fubibinini's Theorem, the order of integration is still irrelevant given the usual terms and conditions.
    • If expressing an integral over some nnn-dimensional region as nnn iterated integrals, the innermost integral can have bounds a,ba, ba,b that are functions of the other n−1n-1n−1 variables, the second-innermost integral can have bounds that are functions of the other n−2n-2n−2 variables, etc., etc., until the outermost integral can only have constant bounds.
    • The Jacobian J(y1,y2,..yn)J(y_1,y_2,..y_n)J(y1​,y2​,..yn​) for a change of variables x1,...,xn→y1,...,ynx_1, ..., x_n \to y_1, ..., y_nx1​,...,xn​→y1​,...,yn​ is given by the matrix determinant ∣∂xi∂yj∣|\frac{\partial x_i}{{\partial y_j}}|∣∂yj​∂xi​​∣.
