Solution Curves

How can we understand the nature of the family of solutions to a differential equation (when the constant term in the solution varies) without actually solving it? The following methods will introduce several key points to plotting such solution curves:

  1. Spotting constant solutions (e.g. y=±1y = \pm 1y=±1)
  2. Analyzing the sign of dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ for x=0,x>0x=0, x>0x=0,x>0 and x<0x<0x<0
  3. Determining stability. (See below)
  4. Finding isoclines: curves along which dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ is constant.

Method. (Isoclines). For a differential equation dydx=...\frac{dy}{dx} = ...dxdy​=..., an isocline is a curve on which dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ is constant, e.g. dydx=1\frac{dy}{dx} = 1dxdy​=1; they can be found simply by setting dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ to that value. (For instance, if dydx=x2+3,x2+3=1  ⟺  x2=−2\frac{dy}{dx}=x^2+3, x^2+3=1 \iff x^2 = -2dxdy​=x2+3,x2+3=1⟺x2=−2 is the curve on which dydx=1\frac{dy}{dx}=1dxdy​=1.)

Isoclines are useful in plotting solution curves because they can be plotted with a slope field, i.e. at every point on the isocline, its slope can be drawn as an arrow. The solution curves to the DE must have a shape that follow these arrows.
