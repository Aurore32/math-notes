Green's Theorem

Theorem. Green's Theorem. Suppose that F=(P(x,y),Q(x,y))\mathbf{F} = (P(x,y),Q(x,y))F=(P(x,y),Q(x,y)) is a vector field in R2\mathbb{R^2}R2 where both P(x,y)P(x,y)P(x,y) and Q(x,y)Q(x,y)Q(x,y) are smooth functions of xxx and yyy. Then for a region AAA in R2\mathbb{R^2}R2 and its (closed) boundary curve CCC, we have

∫A(∂Q∂x−∂P∂y) dA=∮CP dx+Q dy\int_A (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y})\ dA = \oint_C P\ dx + Q\ dy∫A​(∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​) dA=∮C​P dx+Q dy

where the right-hand side can also be rewritten ∮CF⋅ds.\oint_C \mathbf{F\cdot ds}.∮C​F⋅ds.

The big idea

The idea behind Green's theorem is not at all obvious from the equation given: in reality, it relates microscopic circulation (the little swirlies) to macroscopic circulation (the big swirly).

The right-hand side of the given statement is perhaps easier explained: ∮CF⋅ds\oint_C \mathbf{F \cdot ds}∮C​F⋅ds is an integral over the projection of the vector field F\mathbf{F}F in the direction of the curve CCC, resulting in the total magnitude of F\mathbf{F}F that aligns with the direction of CCC - the circulation of F\mathbf{F}F about CCC. Call this the macroscopic circulation, so named because it represents the circulation over the entire boundary of the curve (helpful graphic below).

On the other hand, what do we make of ∫A(∂Q∂x−∂P∂y) dA\int_A (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y})\ dA∫A​(∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​) dA? Clearly this is a sum of some quantity within the region bounded by the curve, but what exactly does

∂Q∂x−∂P∂y\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​

represent?

We will soon see that this quantity is, in reality, a representation of curl - and thus rotation - in two dimensions; but as that fact is far from immediately clear, we argue from another perspective. At every point, the vector field represented by

N=(Q,−P)\mathbf{N} = (Q, -P)N=(Q,−P)

is normal to F=(P,Q)\mathbf{F} = (P,Q)F=(P,Q); the expression ∂Q∂x−∂P∂y\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​ can thus be interpreted as the divergence ∇⋅N\nabla \cdot \mathbf{N}∇⋅N of the normal vector field to F\mathbf{F}F. Tangents to a curve measure rates of change; normals to a curve measure torsion (and thus rotation).

For a vector field, it helps us sleep at night to think much the same; and so it shall be, for the time being, that the divergence of the normal vector field - the flow of F\mathbf{F}F in the normal direction - represents the rotation of the curve. Call these infinitesimally little rotations - the amount to which the curve rotates, or circulates, at each point in the region AAA - the microscopic circulation:

Green's theorem thus tells us that the microscopic circulation of a vector field F\mathbf{F}F in a region AAA - the circulation at every point - is equal to the macroscopic rotation of F\mathbf{F}F around the boundary curve CCC of AAA. Or, in much more eloquent terms:

The sum of all of the little swirlies on the inside is equal to the big swirly on the outside.

This statement is remarkable not only because of its concision, but also - much like the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and the Divergence Theorem after it - because it states that to understand what happens inside a region (or straight line on an axis, or some vaguely humanoid 3D solid), we need only to look at what happens on its boundary. This is true for many things, like the head of cabbage I've kept on my shelf for seven months straight. Or Lord Voldemort. Or the US, if you're a Fox News reporter.

Sketching a proof

The highlighting is entirely necessary to ensure finer absorption of knowledge.

In the case where we're gallivanting about a simply-connected region (i.e. a McMuffin instead of a bagel), Green's Theorem is rather convenient to prove with the Divergence Theorem in our arsenal. In the two-dimensional case of the Divergence Theorem, we have
