Vector Product

Similar to the scalar product, we can also define a binary operation known as the vector product over a vector space that takes two vectors and outputs another vector:

Definition (Vector product). For vectors a,b∈R3\mathbf{a, b} \in \mathbb{R^3}a,b∈R3, define the vector product between them as

a×b=∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ n^\mathbf{a\times b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin \theta\ \mathbf{\hat{n}}a×b=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ n^

Where n^\mathbf{\hat{n}}n^ is the unit vector in the direction normal to both a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b, and θ\thetaθ is the angle between the two vectors.

Essentially, the vector product takes two vectors and outputs another vector with length equal to the product of their lengths times sin⁡θ\sin \thetasinθ, and in a direction normal to both of them. To make such an output unique, we restrict this direction to be the normal direction to a,b\mathbf{a, b}a,b in a right-handed sense:

The vector product satisfies the following properties:

  1. a×b=b×a\mathbf{a\times b = b\times a}a×b=b×a.
  2. a×a=0\mathbf{a \times a = 0}a×a=0.
  3. a×b=0  ⟺  a=λb\mathbf{a \times b = 0}\iff \mathbf{a} = \lambda \mathbf{b}a×b=0⟺a=λb for some λ∈R\lambda \in \mathbb{R}λ∈R, or b=0\mathbf{b}= 0b=0
  4. a×λb=λ(a×b)\mathbf{a \times}\lambda\mathbf{b} = \lambda(\mathbf{a\times b})a×λb=λ(a×b)
  5. a×(b+c)=a×b+a×c\mathbf{a}\times \mathbf{(b + c)} = \mathbf{a\times b + a \times c}a×(b+c)=a×b+a×c.

The following sections will demonstrate three nifty applications of the vector product.

Area of a triangle

If vectors a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b are two sides a triangle (with b−a\mathbf{b-a}b−a as the third side), then we know that the area of the triangle is half the product of the lengths of two sides times the sine of the angle between them:

S=12∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ.S = \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin \theta.S=21​∣a∣∣b∣sinθ.

However, the magnitude of the vector product itself is simply

∣a×b∣=∣∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ n^∣=∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ∣n^∣=∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ|\mathbf{a\times b}| = ||\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin \theta\ \mathbf{\hat{n}}| = |a||b|\sin \theta |\mathbf{\hat{n}}| = |a||b|\sin \theta∣a×b∣=∣∣a∣∣b∣sinθ n^∣=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ∣n^∣=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ

as n^\hat{n}n^ is a unit vector. Thus, the area of the triangle is half the magnitude of the vector product.

Area of a parallelpiped

(Derived from the Ancient Greek prefix parallel, meaning parallel, and pipos, meaning pipes.)

A parallelpiped is just a really jiggly parallelogram. (This should be a proposition.) We first define

Definition (Scalar triple product). Write [a,b,c][\mathbf{a,b,c}][a,b,c] for three vectors a,b,c\mathbf{a,b,c}a,b,c to denote the product a⋅(b×c)\mathbf{a\cdot(b\times c)}a⋅(b×c). As this is a vector product first and a scalar product second, this outputs a scalar.

We propose that

Proposition. A parallelpiped is just a really jiggly parallelogram. A parallelpiped with sides a,b,c\mathbf{a,b,c}a,b,c, which form a right-handed system as shown above, has volume equal to [a,b,c][\mathbf{a,b,c}][a,b,c].

Proof. The volume of this parallelopiped is the area of the parallelogram encompassed by b,c\mathbf{b,c}b,c multiplied by its height.

First, the area of that parallelogram is ∣b×c∣|\mathbf{b\times c}|∣b×c∣ - double the area of a triangle, which has area 12∣b×c∣\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{b\times c}|21​∣b×c∣ as shown above.

We note that the height of the parallelpiped is ∣a∣cos⁡θ|\mathbf{a}|\cos \theta∣a∣cosθ, where θ\thetaθ is the angle between a\mathbf{a}a and the direction normal to the base - thus, the direction normal to both b\mathbf{b}b and c\mathbf{c}c.

This direction is given by the dot product a⋅(b×c)\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b\times c})a⋅(b×c), as it equals ∣a∣∣b×c∣cos⁡θ|\mathbf{a||b\times c|}\cos \theta∣a∣∣b×c∣cosθ where θ\thetaθ is exactly the angle we are looking for. This completes the proof.

A final note on the vector product in 2D and 3D

In 3D (vectors in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3), the vector product is all fine and dandy; it has a geometric interpretation as a vector perpendicular to two vectors, and it outputs a vector. This definition completely collapses when we reach a different dimension, like my mental health after doing too many seeded Minecraft speedruns in an hour. As such, in 2D we simply define

a×b=∣a∣∣b∣sin⁡θ.\mathbf{a\times b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta.a×b=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ.
