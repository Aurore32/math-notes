Directional Derivative and Gradient

Before we can be concerned with differential equations, we have to concern ourselves with rates of change.

Definition. (Gradient for multivariate functions). Define ∇f\nabla f∇f, the \it gradient \normalfont of f(x,y)=zf(x,y)=zf(x,y)=z, as the vector

∇f=[∂f∂x∂f∂y]\nabla f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \end{bmatrix}∇f=[∂x∂f​∂y∂f​​]

and for the generalized multivariate case, a column vector of partial derivatives of fff with respect to all its variables.

Note that this is simply a definition; we haven't attached any geometric significance to the gradient yet, but we will - eventually.

To get there, consider the following question. We know that ∂f∂y\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}∂y∂f​ gives us the rate of change of fff with small changes in xxx, and the partial derivative in yyy with small changes in yyy; however, what if we choose to move not along one of the axes, but (as is possible in three dimensions) along a certain vector? For instance, take the unit vector

s⃗=[ab]\vec{s} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}s=[ab​]

for some aaa, bbb with a2+b2=1\sqrt{a^2+b^2}=1a2+b2​=1. If we're going to be moving along s⃗\vec{s}s, then we'll be moving aaa units in the xxx-direction and bbb units in the yyy-direction. The partial derivatives give us how much fff changes when we move along each direction, so we can write

∂f∂s=a∂f∂x+b∂f∂y\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = a\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + b\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}∂s∂f​=a∂x∂f​+b∂y∂f​

as the rate of change of fff along s⃗\vec{s}s. Note that we can also rewrite the above as

∂f∂s⃗=∇f⋅s⃗ (notated ∇s⃗f)\frac{\partial f}{\partial \vec{s}} = \nabla f \cdot \vec{s} \ (\text{notated }\nabla_{\vec{s}} f)∂s∂f​=∇f⋅s (notated ∇s​f)

which gives us an insight on what the gradient represents: essentially, it is a vector representing the "base" rate of change at a point that all other directional derivatives are based off of.

We also note that, since the dot product is maximized when cos⁡θ=1\cos \theta = 1cosθ=1 and the angle θ\thetaθ between the two vectors equalling zero, the directional derivative is at a maximum when s⃗\vec{s}s, the direction travelled in, is in the same direction as the gradient. Thus, the gradient is the direction that maximizes the rate of change of the function. If instead s⃗\vec{s}s is parallel to the gradient, then the directional derivative is zero.

Theorem. The gradient ∇f\nabla f∇f of a multivariate function fff at a certain point is a vector that geometrically symbolizes the direction of greatest rate of change of fff from that point.
