Forms

What are forms?

Definition. A form F(x)F(\mathbf{x})F(x), with associated coefficient matrix AAA (or F(x)\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x})F(x), which has a funny squiggly little tail and is ten times more of a pain in my ass when trying to type this up in LaTeX), is defined as the linear map

F(x)=x†Ax=∑i=1n∑j=1nxi∗AijxjF(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^{\dagger} A \mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^n x^{*}_i A_{ij}x_jF(x)=x†Ax=i=1∑n​j=1∑n​xi∗​Aij​xj​

which is the complex scalar product between x\mathbf{x}x and the image of x\mathbf{x}x under AAA, AxA\mathbf{x}Ax. The map FFF maps the n×1n\times 1n×1 vector x\mathbf{x}x to a complex scalar in C\mathbb{C}C: Cn→C\mathbb{C^n \to C}Cn→C.

Definition. If the coefficient matrix of a form FFF is Hermitian, FFF is called a Hermitian form.

Property. Hermitian forms have real outputs; they map to R\mathbb{R}R instead of C\mathbb{C}C.

Proof.

Consider the form F(x)F(\mathbf{x})F(x) with Hermitian coefficient matrix HHH. We have

F(x)=x†HxF(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^{\dagger} H \mathbf{x}F(x)=x†Hx

which, in order to prove is real, requires

F(x)†=F(x)∗=F(x)F(\mathbf{x})^{\dagger} = F(\mathbf{x})^{*} = F(\mathbf{x})F(x)†=F(x)∗=F(x)

as the complex conjugate of a scalar is itself if and only if the scalar is real. This implies that

(x†Hx)†=x†H†x=x†Hx(\mathbf{x}^{\dagger} H \mathbf{x})^\dagger = \mathbf{x}^{\dagger} H^\dagger \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^{\dagger} H \mathbf{x}(x†Hx)†=x†H†x=x†Hx

which is true as H†=HH^{\dagger} = HH†=H by property of a Hermitian matrix.

Definition. If the coefficient matrix of a form FFF is a real symmetric matrix, FFF is called a quadratic form.

Principle axes and simplifying forms

This represents a general quadratic polynomial as it encompasses the second-order terms (x1xj)(x_1x_j)(x1​xj​), the first-order terms (xi)(x_i)(xi​), and the constant term ccc. In matrix form, we can write the above as

xTAx+bTx+c=0.\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x + b^T x} + c = 0.xTAx+bTx+c=0.

Application. Conic sections.

Definition. Conic sections are a special case of quadric surfaces: specifically, those in 2 variables (e.g. xxx and yyy).

Proposition. Conic sections have general form

λ1(x′)2+λ2(y′)2=k,\lambda_1 (x')^2 + \lambda_2 (y')^2 = k,λ1​(x′)2+λ2​(y′)2=k,

(x′)2λ2+(y′)2λ1=k\frac{(x')^2}{\lambda_2} + \frac{(y')^2}{\lambda_1} = kλ2​(x′)2​+λ1​(y′)2​=k

for any constant kkk. Note that x′x'x′ and y′y'y′ are intentionally used to indicate that they can be different orthogonal axes than the default xxx- and yyy-axes. This derives from the general form of quadric sections yTΛy=k\mathbf{y}^T\Lambda\mathbf{y}=kyTΛy=k with eigenvectors λ1,λ2\lambda_1, \lambda_2λ1​,λ2​, which holds true as long as λ1≠0\lambda_1 \neq 0λ1​=0 and λ2≠0\lambda_2 \neq 0λ2​=0.

Conic sections (so-called because can be classified into the following forms according to the values of λ1\lambda_1λ1​ and λ2\lambda_2λ2​:

  1. Ellipses. If λ1λ2>0\lambda_1\lambda_2 > 0λ1​λ2​>0, the two eigenvalues are of the same sign. As such, the equation can be manipulated to yield

    (x′)2a2+(y′)2b2=1\frac{(x')^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y')^2}{b^2}=1a2(x′)2​+b2(y′)2​=1

    for some aaa, bbb, which determines an ellipse with width 2a2a2a and height 2b2b2b about the origin. Its principal axes are given by the x′x'x′ and y′y'y′ axes (which are the eigenvectors of the coefficient matrix of the original form), and its scale is determined by kkk. If a=ba = ba=b or λ1=λ2\lambda_1 = \lambda_2λ1​=λ2​, the ellipse becomes a circle; any pair of orthogonal principal axes can be chosen.

  2. Hyperbolas. If λ1λ2<0\lambda_1 \lambda_2 < 0λ1​λ2​<0, the conic section is a hyperbola with principal axes coinciding with the x′x'x′- and y′y'y′-axes.

  3. Parabolas. If λ1=0\lambda_1 = 0λ1​=0 or λ2=0\lambda_2 = 0λ2​=0 (but not both at the same time), Λ\LambdaΛ is not invertible; instead, an alternate translation gives a conic section of the form λ1(x′)2+b2′y2=k\lambda_1 (x')^2 + b_2' y_2 = kλ1​(x′)2+b2′​y2​=k, which describes a (quadratic) parabola with principal axes coinciding with the x′x'x′- and y′y'y′-axes.

Theorem. (Principal axis theorem). From the fact that symmetric matrices have orthogonal eigenvectors, we observe that the principal axes of any conic section are orthogonal.
