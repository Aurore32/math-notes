Change of Basis

Why change basis?

Is there an ideal type of matrix that's the easiest to work with: that's trivially easy to multiply, to take exponents of, to add, etc., and that we can convert all matrices to? The answer is yes - diagonal matrices satisfy all those properties - and the central purpose of changing basis for linear maps is to express them through the lens of such an easily-workable matrix.

Definition. A diagonal matrix is a matrix DDD with all elements not on its diagonal equal to zero: more precisely, we have D={Dij}D = \{D_{ij}\}D={Dij​} and Dij=0D_{ij} = 0Dij​=0 whenever i≠ji \neq ji=j. As such, DDD will look something like this:

D=[D1100…00D220…000D33…0000⋱0000…Dnn]D = \begin{bmatrix} D_{11} & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & D_{22} & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & D_{33} & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & D_{nn} \end{bmatrix}D=⎣⎡​D11​0000​0D22​000​00D33​00​………⋱…​0000Dnn​​⎦⎤​

or, in Delta notation, we have D={Diiδij}D = \{D_{ii} \delta_{ij}\}D={Dii​δij​} (not invoking suffix/summation convention).

Property. Diagonal matrices are extremely convenient to work around in part because it is easy to take their exponents. For instance, we have

(D2)ij=∑kDiiδikDkkδkj=Dii2δij2=(D)ij2(D^2)_{ij} = \sum_{k}D_{ii}\delta_{ik}D_{kk}\delta_{kj} = D_{ii}^2 \delta_{ij} ^2 = (D)_{ij}^2(D2)ij​=k∑​Dii​δik​Dkk​δkj​=Dii2​δij2​=(D)ij2​

or, in other words, we have

D2=[D11200…00D2220…000D332…0000⋱0000…Dnn2]D^2 = \begin{bmatrix} D_{11}^2 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & D_{22}^2 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & D_{33}^2 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & D_{nn}^2 \end{bmatrix}D2=⎣⎡​D112​0000​0D222​000​00D332​00​………⋱…​0000Dnn2​​⎦⎤​

and similarly

Dn=[D11n00…00D22n0…000D33n…0000⋱0000…Dnnn]D^n = \begin{bmatrix} D_{11}^n & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & D_{22}^n & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & D_{33}^n & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & D_{nn}^n \end{bmatrix}Dn=⎣⎡​D11n​0000​0D22n​000​00D33n​00​………⋱…​0000Dnnn​​⎦⎤​

Eigenvectors as a Change of Basis

Up until this point, we've made passing references and sly allusions to the fact that the matrix representation for a linear map AAA is "in terms of the standard basis". If we want to change the basis for that matrix representation, do we change the linear map itself?

The answer is no. The linear map itself does not change - if (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) was mapped to (4,3)(4,3)(4,3) before, so will it remain even after a change of basis. But what will change is the matrix representation of that linear map; and as we will see, some bases result in far simpler matrices than others.

Theorem. Suppose that the linear map A:Fn→FnA: \mathbb{F^n} \to \mathbb{F^n}A:Fn→Fn has nnn linearly independent eigenvectors x1,x2,...,xn\mathbf{x_1, x_2, ..., x_n}x1​,x2​,...,xn​. Then using these eigenvectors as a basis for its matrix representation results in a diagonal matrix for AAA.

Proof. What we mean by a change of basis is as follows. Any vector x∈Fn\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{F}^nx∈Fn can be written in terms of a linear combination of the standard basis (1,0,...,0),...,(0,...,0,1)=e1,...,en(1,0,...,0),...,(0,...,0,1) = e_1, ..., e_n(1,0,...,0),...,(0,...,0,1)=e1​,...,en​. As previously defined, the matrix representation of a linear map AAA in terms of the standard basis is

[A(e1)A(e2)...A(en)]\begin{bmatrix} A(e_1) & A(e_2) & ... & A(e_n) \end{bmatrix}[A(e1​)​A(e2​)​...​A(en​)​]

where the columns are e1,...,ene_1, ..., e_ne1​,...,en​ under transformation by AAA. If we instead use the basis of eigenvectors x1,x2,...,xn\mathbf{x_1}, \mathbf{x_2}, ..., \mathbf{x_n}x1​,x2​,...,xn​, which span Fn\mathbb{F^n}Fn, we obtain the matrix representation

[A(x1)A(x2)...A(xn)]\begin{bmatrix} A(\mathbf{x}_1) & A(\mathbf{x}_2) & ... & A(\mathbf{x}_n) \end{bmatrix}[A(x1​)​A(x2​)​...​A(xn​)​]

which, as A(xi)=λixiA(\mathbf{x_i}) = \lambda_i \mathbf{x}_iA(xi​)=λi​xi​ by definition, yields

[λ1(x1)λ2(x2)...λn(xn)]\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1(\mathbf{x}_1) & \lambda_2(\mathbf{x}_2) & ... & \lambda_n(\mathbf{x}_n) \end{bmatrix}[λ1​(x1​)​λ2​(x2​)​...​λn​(xn​)​]

However, as we are using x1,x2,...,xn\mathbf{x_1}, \mathbf{x_2}, ..., \mathbf{x_n}x1​,x2​,...,xn​ as our basis, each column must be expressed in terms of a linear combination of these vectors:

{λ1x1=λ1x1+0x2+...+0xnλ2x2=0x1+λ2x2+...+0xn⋮\begin{cases} \lambda_1\mathbf{x_1} = \lambda_1\mathbf{x_1} + 0\mathbf{x_2} + ... + 0\mathbf{x_n} \\ \lambda_2\mathbf{x_2} = 0\mathbf{x_1} + \lambda_2\mathbf{x_2} + ... + 0\mathbf{x_n} \\ \vdots \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​λ1​x1​=λ1​x1​+0x2​+...+0xn​λ2​x2​=0x1​+λ2​x2​+...+0xn​⋮​

This yields the matrix

A=[λ100...00λ20...000λ3...0000⋱0000…λn]A = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & 0 & ... & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & 0 & ... & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_3 & ... & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}A=⎣⎡​λ1​0000​0λ2​000​00λ3​00​.........⋱…​0000λn​​⎦⎤​

which is diagonal.

On a more intuitive level: the basis for a linear map is the "axes" on which it is acting; if we choose the standard basis, for instance, these axes are simply, in the case of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, the x-, y-, and z-axis. Eigenvectors are vectors along which the linear map preserves direction; if you apply the linear map to its eigenvector, you get a constant multiple of that eigenvector. Thus, if you let the eigenvectors of the linear map be its axes, then all it does along each axis is scale that axis by a constant multiple - yielding a diagonal matrix, as above.

Such a change of basis - transforming an ordinarily complex matrix into a simple diagonal one - can be useful in the case of repeated applications of the linear map; as exponentiating a diagonal matrix is relatively painless, if we apply AAA multiple times with eigenvectors as the basis, then our result should also be relatively simple.

Change of Basis: The General Formula
