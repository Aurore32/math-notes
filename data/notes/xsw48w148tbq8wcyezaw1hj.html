Eigenspaces

Definition. Define the set of all vectors corresponding to an eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ as the eigenspace of λ\lambdaλ, denoted EλE_{\lambda}Eλ​. As this set is the nullspace of (A−λI)(A-\lambda I)(A−λI), it is a subspace.

Definition. Define the algebraic multiplicity MλM_{\lambda}Mλ​ of an eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ of a matrix AAA as the multiplicity of the root λ\lambdaλ in the characteristic equation of AAA.

Property. For any matrix AAA with characteristic polynomial of degree nnn, summing across all its eigenvalues yields

∑λMλ=n.\sum_{\lambda} M_{\lambda} = n.λ∑​Mλ​=n.

We refer to any eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ with Mλ>1M_{\lambda} > 1Mλ​>1 as degenerate.

Definition. Define the geometric multiplicity mλm_{\lambda}mλ​ of λ\lambdaλ as the dimension of its eigenspace: mλ=dim⁡Eλm_{\lambda} = \dim E_{\lambda}mλ​=dimEλ​. (This is equivalent to the number of linearly independent eigenvectors corresponding to λ\lambdaλ: the number of independent solutions to (A−λI)x=0(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{x=0}(A−λI)x=0.)

Theorem. For any eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ, we have mλ≤Mλm_{\lambda} \leq M_{\lambda}mλ​≤Mλ​: the geometric multiplicity is no greater than the algebraic multiplicity.

Proof will be shown later.

Definition. Define the defect of λ\lambdaλ as Δλ=Mλ−mλ\Delta_{\lambda} = M_{\lambda} - m_{\lambda}Δλ​=Mλ​−mλ​. As given by the above theorem, the defect is always non-negative.

Property. If ∑λΔλ\sum_{\lambda} \Delta_{\lambda}∑λ​Δλ​ is positive, i.e. if ∑λmλ<n\sum_{\lambda} m_{\lambda} < n∑λ​mλ​<n, then the eigenvectors of AAA do not span its donamin Fn\mathbb{F}^nFn. This is because the eigenspaces corresponding to AAA have total dimension less than nnn.

Definition. Define a generalized eigenvector x\mathbf{x}x of a linear map (matrix) A:Fn→FnA: \mathbb{F^n}\to\mathbb{F^n}A:Fn→Fn as satisfying the equation

(A−λI)kx=0,(A-\lambda I)^k\mathbf{x=0},(A−λI)kx=0,

where λ\lambdaλ is the corresponding eigenvalue and kkk is a positive integer. Generalized eigenvectors are necessary when degenerate eigenvalues exist; i.e. the eigenvectors do not span Fn\mathbb{F}^nFn. In such a case, the generalized eigenvectors do span Fn\mathbb{F}^nFn and the behavior of AAA can be analyzed through them.

But even if no eigenvalue is defective - i.e. the dimensions of all the eigenspaces sum to nnn - can we guarantee that the eigenvectors of AAA span its domain? The following theorem ensures it is so:

An alternative, and much shorter, proof is found below:

Proof. As above, let xi\mathbf{x_i}xi​ be the eigenvectors of AAA and λi\lambda_iλi​ be their corresponding eigenvalues for i=1,2,...,ri = 1, 2, ..., ri=1,2,...,r. Let

Remarks.

  1. As all eigenvectors are linearly independent, if a n×nn\times nn×n matrix has nnn distinct eigenvectors, its eigenvectors span Fn\mathbb{F^n}Fn.
  2. A n×nn\times nn×n matrix can have at most nnn distinct eigenvectors, as its domain has dimension nnn.
  3. If a n×nn\times nn×n matrix does not have nnn distinct eigenvalues, it is sometimes still possible to find nnn distinct eigenvectors that span its domain.
