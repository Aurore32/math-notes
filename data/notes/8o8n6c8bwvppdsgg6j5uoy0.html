Existence and Uniqueness

Boundary conditions and Poisson's Equation

Suppose we want to solve the Poisson equation

∇2Φ=ρ(x)\nabla^2 \Phi = \rho(\mathbf{x})∇2Φ=ρ(x)

for some source function ρ\rhoρ, and within a region of space VVV with boundary ∂V\partial V∂V. In particular, we specify one of two boundary conditions over ∂V\partial V∂V: either

Definition. The Dirichlet condition, for which Φ(x)\Phi(\mathbf{x})Φ(x) is directly specified by a function ψ(x)\psi(\mathbf{x})ψ(x) for some ψ\psiψ on all points on ∂V\partial V∂V: Ψ(x)=ψ(x),x∈∂V\Psi(\mathbf{x}) = \psi(\mathbf{x}), \mathbf{x} \in \partial VΨ(x)=ψ(x),x∈∂V.

or, alternatively,

Definition. The Neumann condition, for which the directional derivative in the direction of the normal n\mathbf{n}n to the surface ∂V\partial V∂V is specified by a function ψ(x)\psi(\mathbf{x})ψ(x): Φ(x)⋅n=ψ(x),x∈∂V\mathbf{\Phi(x)\cdot n} = \psi(\mathbf{x}), \mathbf{x}\in\partial VΦ(x)⋅n=ψ(x),x∈∂V.

For this broad formulation of boundary-value problems concerning Poisson's equation, we claim an equally broad statement of uniqueness:

Theorem. The solution Φ\PhiΦ to the Poisson equation ∇2Φ=ρ\nabla^2\Phi = \rho∇2Φ=ρ over a bounded region VVV, where either the Dirichlet or the Neumann condition is specified over the boundary ∂V\partial V∂V, is unique as long as it exists.

Proof.

Crucial to proving this statement is the following lemma, known as Green's first identity, named (much like Green's theorem and Green's second identity) after the Green Goblin:

Lemma. For scalar fields ϕ\phiϕ and ψ\psiψ in R3\mathbb{R^3}R3, we have

∫Vϕ∇2ψ dV=∫∂Vϕ∇ψ⋅dS−∫V∇ϕ⋅∇ψ dV.\int_V \phi \nabla^2 \psi\ dV = \int_{\partial V}\phi\nabla\psi\cdot\mathbf{dS} - \int_V \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla \psi\ dV.∫V​ϕ∇2ψ dV=∫∂V​ϕ∇ψ⋅dS−∫V​∇ϕ⋅∇ψ dV.

This can be understood as a multivariate analogue to integration by parts:

∫u dv=uv−∫v du\int u\ dv = uv - \int v\ du∫u dv=uv−∫v du

in which we have u=ϕu = \phiu=ϕ and v=∇ψv = \nabla \psiv=∇ψ, and the surface integral can be understood as "reducing the degree of integration" by 111.

Proof.

This lemma follows directly from the application of the Divergence Theorem on the surface integral:

∫∂Vϕ∇ψ⋅dS=∫∂V∇⋅(ϕ∇ψ)dV\int_{\partial V}\phi \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_{\partial V} \nabla\cdot(\phi \nabla \psi) dV∫∂V​ϕ∇ψ⋅dS=∫∂V​∇⋅(ϕ∇ψ)dV

resulting in the other two terms. Correspondingly, we also have the result

∫Vϕ∇2ψ−ψ∇2ϕ dV=∫∂V(ϕ∇ψ−ψ∇ϕ)⋅dS;\int_V \phi\nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi\ dV = \int_{\partial V}(\phi \nabla \psi - \psi \nabla \phi) \cdot \mathbf{dS};∫V​ϕ∇2ψ−ψ∇2ϕ dV=∫∂V​(ϕ∇ψ−ψ∇ϕ)⋅dS;

this is known as Green's second identity, no relation to the first.

After the preceding contractually-obligated commercial break, we now return to the Poisson equation. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that both Φ\PhiΦ and Ψ\PsiΨ are scalar fields that satisfy a given boundary condition and solve the Poisson equation over any arbitrary region VVV. Then we have

∇2Φ−∇2Ψ=∇2(Φ−Ψ)=ρ−ρ=0\nabla^2 \Phi - \nabla^2 \Psi = \nabla^2 (\Phi - \Psi) = \rho - \rho = 0∇2Φ−∇2Ψ=∇2(Φ−Ψ)=ρ−ρ=0

with Φ−Ψ\Phi - \PsiΦ−Ψ satisfying the Laplace equation in the same region; denote Φ−Ψ\Phi - \PsiΦ−Ψ as fff. By Green's first identity, we have

∫Vf∇2f dV=∫V0 dV=0\int_V f\nabla^2 f\ dV = \int_V 0\ dV = 0∫V​f∇2f dV=∫V​0 dV=0

on the left-hand side, taking ϕ=ψ=f\phi = \psi = fϕ=ψ=f, and on the right-hand side

∫∂Vf∇f⋅dS−∫V(∇f)⋅(∇f) dV\int_{\partial V} f\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{dS} - \int_V (\nabla f) \cdot (\nabla f)\ dV∫∂V​f∇f⋅dS−∫V​(∇f)⋅(∇f) dV

resulting in

∫∂Vf∇f⋅dS=∫V(∇f)⋅(∇f) dV=∫V∣∇f∣2 dV\int_{\partial V} f\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_V (\nabla f) \cdot (\nabla f)\ dV = \int_V |\nabla f|^2\ dV∫∂V​f∇f⋅dS=∫V​(∇f)⋅(∇f) dV=∫V​∣∇f∣2 dV

which we note is always non-negative.

Now we make use of the boundary conditions. If both Φ\PhiΦ and Ψ\PsiΨ satisfy either the Dirichlet or Neumann boundary conditions, then either Φ−Ψ=f=0\Phi - \Psi = f = 0Φ−Ψ=f=0 (Dirichlet; both equal the same function on the boundary) or ∇(Φ−Ψ)⋅n=∇f⋅n=0\nabla(\Phi - \Psi) \cdot \mathbf{n} = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0∇(Φ−Ψ)⋅n=∇f⋅n=0 (Neumann; the gradient of both fields have the same dot product with the normal to the boundary).

As such, the left-hand side above is zero in both cases; for the Dirichlet condition f=0f = 0f=0 directly, and for the Neumann condition ∇f⋅dS=∇f⋅n dS=0\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{dS} = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{n}\ dS = 0∇f⋅dS=∇f⋅n dS=0. However, this surface integral - which is always zero - always equals the integral of a non-negative quantity ∣∇f∣2|\nabla f|^2∣∇f∣2 over any arbitrary volume; as any volume can be chosen, this clearly reaches a contradiction. □\square□

We have two small amendments to make to the above theorem before proceeding:

  1. The above proof is just as viable for boundary conditions that are a mix of Neumann and Dirichlet conditions, as well as for regions where there are different boundary conditions on different parts of the boundary.

  2. Uniqueness does not guarantee existence even though existence guarantees uniqueness. For instance, if Φ\PhiΦ satisfies ∇2Φ=ρ\nabla^2 \Phi = \rho∇2Φ=ρ in a region VVV with Neumann condition ∇Φ⋅n=g(x)\nabla \Phi \cdot \mathbf{n} = g(\mathbf{x})∇Φ⋅n=g(x) for some ggg, then

    ∯∂V∇Φ⋅n dS=∯∂V∇Φ⋅dS=∫V∇2Φ dV\oiint_{\partial V} \nabla \Phi \cdot \mathbf{n}\ dS = \oiint_{\partial V} \nabla \Phi \cdot \mathbf{dS} = \int_V \nabla^2\Phi\ dV∬​∂V​∇Φ⋅n dS=∬​∂V​∇Φ⋅dS=∫V​∇2Φ dV

    by the Divergence Theorem, which mandates that ∯∂Vg dS=∫Vρ dV\oiint_{\partial V} g\ dS = \int_V \rho\ dV∬​∂V​g dS=∫V​ρ dV.

Harmonic functions and their properties

Definition. Solutions to the Laplace equation ∇2ψ=0\nabla^2 \psi = 0∇2ψ=0 are called harmonic functions. Not to be confused with the type of harmonic function that pops up in a musicology PhD's doctoral thesis analyzing the first bars of Rick Astley's hit single, "Never Gonna Give You Up".

Harmonic functions pop up everywhere in the real world. As stated, they describe the potential function of something when that particular "something" has no source - when it is equally diffused throughout the region, like heat or fluid flow or "the smell of that guy who hasn't showered in a week showing up to the lecture hall".

This section will introduce just two of the countless fascinating properties of harmonic functions. (In reality the only two fascinating properties, but we don't need to know that.)

Property 2. The extreme-value property. Harmonic functions satisfying the Laplace equation within a region VVV have no local maxima or minima within VVV; any maxima or minima must be on the boundary of the region.
