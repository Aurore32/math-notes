∇f=0⃗\nabla f = \vec{0}∇f=0

Definition. A saddle point, analogously to points of inflection for single-variable functions, is a point where the gradient is zero but the function is not locally maximized or minimized.

Why does this hold true? It is beyond our abilities to provide a full proof at the moment, but using the tools of eigenvalues in linear algebra, we can provide an intuitive explanation for all of this.

We first note that HHH is "symmetric" no matter how many dimensions or variables there are; when we flip it around the diagonal, it remains the same because for a continuous function, fxy=fyxf_{xy}=f_{yx}fxy​=fyx​. (This symmetry is most evident in the two-dimensional case).

A result from linear algebra tells us that because of this symmetry, HHH can be diagonalized, i.e. it can be written in terms of its eigenvalues:
