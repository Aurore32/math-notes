  3. Reflection in the plane x⋅n=0\mathbf{x \cdot n} = 0x⋅n=0 in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, where n\mathbf{n}n is a unit normal vector. Suppose we are reflecting a point with position vector p=OP⃗\mathbf{p} = \vec{OP}p=OP about the plane. Then its image after reflection, P′P'P′, is connected to PPP by two times the normal vector from PPP to the plane. Call the foot of this normal NNN. Then:

    OP′⃗=OP⃗+PN⃗+NP′⃗=OP⃗−2NP⃗\vec{OP'} = \vec{OP} + \vec{PN} + \vec{NP'} = \vec{OP} - 2 \vec{NP}OP′=OP+PN+NP′=OP−2NP

    which equals

    p−2NP⃗\mathbf{p} - 2\vec{NP}p−2NP

    where NP⃗\vec{NP}NP is (p⋅n)n(\mathbf{p\cdot n})\mathbf{n}(p⋅n)n.

    Proof. NP⃗\vec{NP}NP must be parallel to n\mathbf{n}n as it is normal to the plane; suppose it is equal to dnd\mathbf{n}dn for some scalar ddd. Then as NNN lies on the plane, we have ON⃗=OP⃗−NP⃗=p−dn\vec{ON} = \vec{OP} - \vec{NP} = \mathbf{p} - d\mathbf{n}ON=OP−NP=p−dn and ON⃗⋅n=0\vec{ON}\cdot \mathbf{n} = 0ON⋅n=0 by the equation of the plane. Thus (p−dn)⋅n=0(\mathbf{p}-d\mathbf{n})\cdot \mathbf{n} = 0(p−dn)⋅n=0, yielding d=p⋅nd = \mathbf{p \cdot n}d=p⋅n.

    Using suffix notation, p−2(p⋅n)n\mathbf{p} - 2(\mathbf{p\cdot n})\mathbf{n}p−2(p⋅n)n can be expanded as follows:

    p−2(p⋅n)n=pi−2(pjnj)ni=δijpj−2(pjnj)ni=pj(δij−2njni)=p′\begin{aligned} \mathbf{p} - 2(\mathbf{p\cdot n})\mathbf{n} &= p_{i} - 2(p_jn_j)n_i \\ &= \delta_{ij}p_j - 2(p_jn_j)n_i \\ &= p_j(\delta_{ij}-2n_jn_i) \\ &= \mathbf{p}' \end{aligned}p−2(p⋅n)n​=pi​−2(pj​nj​)ni​=δij​pj​−2(pj​nj​)ni​=pj​(δij​−2nj​ni​)=p′​

    Note the trick of using δij\delta_{ij}δij​ to transform the suffix iii into jjj!

    Therefore, the matrix representing a transformation from p\mathbf{p}p to p′\mathbf{p}'p′, its reflection about a plane with normal vector n\mathbf{n}n, can be expressed by

    R={δij−2njni}.R = \{ \delta_{ij} - 2n_j n_i \}.R={δij​−2nj​ni​}.

  4. Shear in any axis (e.g. the xxx-axis) in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. A shear is any transformation that transforms a vector (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) onto, for instance, (x+λy,y,z)(x+\lambda y, y, z)(x+λy,y,z), preserving the two other coordinates while adding a scalar multiple of another coordinate onto one coordinate.     S=[1λ0010001]S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}S=⎣⎡​100​λ10​001​⎦⎤​
