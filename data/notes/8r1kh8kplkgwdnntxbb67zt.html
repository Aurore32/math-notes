Tensor Notation

Notation (Suffix notation). For vectors v\mathbf{v}v, the suffix vi\mathbf{v}_ivi​ is understood to be the iiith component of v\mathbf{v}v; this enables us to convert vector equations to scalar form, e.g. a=λb\mathbf{a=\lambda b}a=λb for two vectors can be written ai=λbi\mathbf{a_i=\lambda b_i}ai​=λbi​.

Building on this, we introduce a notation that allows us to remove the summation symbol in equations such as e.g. a⋅b=∑aibi\mathbf{a\cdot b}=\sum a_i b_ia⋅b=∑ai​bi​. We simply write, for instance, a⋅b=aibi\mathbf{a \cdot b} = a_i b_ia⋅b=ai​bi​; if the suffix iii is repeated twice in a term, that means "sum over iii" - aibia_i b_iai​bi​ is understood to be ∑i=1naibi\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i∑i=1n​ai​bi​, xkykzcx_k y_k z_cxk​yk​zc​ is understood to be summing over kkk and not ccc, etc. If a suffix appears only once in a term (e.g. aijbjka_{ij}b_{jk}aij​bjk​, for iii and kkk), they are not summed over.

The last example - aijbjka_{ij}b_{jk}aij​bjk​ - is an example of tensors, an important class of mathematical object prevalent in linear algebra:

Definition (Tensors). Tensors are items with more than one index: for instance, aija_{ij}aij​ or bijkb_{ijk}bijk​. They may represent entries in a matrix, or other things entirely. For instance:

Defintion (Kronecker delta). Define the tensor δij\delta_{ij}δij​ to be such that

{δij=1, i=j0 otherwise\begin{cases} \delta_{ij}=1,\ i =j \\ 0 \text{ otherwise} \end{cases}{δij​=1, i=j0 otherwise​

If the Kronecker delta represents the entries of a matrix, then that matrix is zero for i≠ji\neq ji=j (entries not on the diagonal) and one for i=ji = ji=j (on the diagonal). Thus, the matrix represented by the Kronecker delta is an identity matrix.

The Kronecker delta can often be useful in writing suffix notation for vector sums; if it is present in a sum, it will eliminate the terms with unequal suffixes and leave only the terms with equal suffixes. For instance:

  1. aiδij=aja_i \delta_{ij} = a_jai​δij​=aj​ (note that this is a sum, not a single term; we are summing over iii per the above notation)
  2. apδpqbq=apbpa_p \delta_{pq} b_q = a_p b_pap​δpq​bq​=ap​bp​ summing over both ppp and qqq. This expression looks confusing, but remember that apbpa_p b_pap​bp​ denotes a sum over ppp, not a single term.

Definition (Alternating symbol). For numbers (i,j,k)(i,j,k)(i,j,k), define an even permutation as a permutation of (i,j,k)(i, j, k)(i,j,k) that can be reached with an even number of swaps of its elements (zero or two swaps): (i,j,k)(i, j, k)(i,j,k) itself (zero swaps), (k,i,j)(k,i,j)(k,i,j), (j,k,i)(j,k,i)(j,k,i). Define an odd permutation analogously as a permutation that can be reached with an odd number of swaps (one swap): (i,k,j)(i,k,j)(i,k,j), (j,i,k)(j,i,k)(j,i,k), (k,j,i)(k,j,i)(k,j,i). Thus define ϵijk\epsilon_{ijk}ϵijk​, the alternating symbol, as

{ϵijk=1, (i,j,k) is an even permutationϵijk=−1, (i,j,k) is an odd permutation0 otherwise (repeated suffixes)\begin{cases} \epsilon_{ijk}=1, \text{ $(i,j,k)$ is an even permutation} \\ \epsilon_{ijk}=-1, \text{ $(i,j,k)$ is an odd permutation} \\ \text{0 otherwise (repeated suffixes)} \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​ϵijk​=1, (i,j,k) is an even permutationϵijk​=−1, (i,j,k) is an odd permutation0 otherwise (repeated suffixes)​
