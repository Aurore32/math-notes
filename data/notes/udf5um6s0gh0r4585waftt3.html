Matrix classifications

Definition. Symmetric matrices AAA are such that A=ATA = A^TA=AT: symmetry arises across the matrix's diagonal.

Definition. Anti-symmetric or skew-symmetric matrices are such that A=−ATA = -A^TA=−AT. This necessitates that the diagonal elements of the matrix are zero, as any AiiA_{ii}Aii​ lying on the diagonal remain unchanged by the transpose; as such, A=−ATA = -A^TA=−AT implies Aii=−Aii=0A_{ii} = -A_{ii} = 0Aii​=−Aii​=0.

Definition. Hermitian matrices are such that A=A†A = A^{\dagger}A=A†. This implies that the diagonal elements of AAA are all real, as real numbers are their own conjugate.

Definition. Skew-Hermitian matrices are such that A=−A†A = -A^{\dagger}A=−A†. The diagonal elements of such a matrix must be purely imaginary.

Matrix inverses

Definition. For a m×nm\times nm×n matrix AAA, the n×mn\times mn×m matrix BBB deemed the left inverse of AAA is such that

BA=IBA = IBA=I

where III is the n×nn\times nn×n identity matrix; simultaneously, the n×mn\times mn×m matrix CCC deemed the right inverse of AAA is such that

AC=IAC = IAC=I

where III is now the m×mm \times mm×m identity matrix.

Theorem. For a square matrix of dimensions n×nn \times nn×n, the left and right inverses are identical. In fact, this is true for any matrix as long as both the left and right inverses exist.

Proof. By the above, BA=I  ⟺  BAC=C  ⟺  B(AC)=CBA=I \iff BAC = C \iff B(AC)=CBA=I⟺BAC=C⟺B(AC)=C by associativity. As AC=IAC = IAC=I by definition, we have B=CB = CB=C.

Definition. Invertible matrices are square matrices whose inverse exists.

Note that if an inverse of a square matrix, denoted A−1A^{-1}A−1 for the matrix AAA, does exist, then it is necessarily unique (as the right inverse equals the left inverse).

Orthogonal and unitary matrices

Definition. Orthogonal matrices AAA are such that the inverse of AAA is equal to its transpose: AT=A−1A^{T} = A^{-1}AT=A−1.

Proof. Let A(i,∗)A_{(i,*)}A(i,∗)​ and A(j,∗)A_{(j,*)}A(j,∗)​ denote the iiith and jjjth row of the n×nn\times nn×n orthonormal matrix AAA, respectively, where i,ji, ji,j are integers from 111 to nnn. Then the sum of the products of their terms equal

A(i,∗)(A(j,∗))T=(AAT)ijA_{(i,*)} (A_{(j,*)})^T = (AA^{T})_{ij}A(i,∗)​(A(j,∗)​)T=(AAT)ij​

which is 111 if i=ji = ji=j and 000 if i≠ji \neq ji=j, as AAT=IAA^T = IAAT=I. Thus, the dot product between two rows of AAA are 000 if they are two different rows and 111 if they are the same row, matching the definition of an orthonormal set. The proof for columns proceeds similarly.

Property. If AAA is an orthogonal matrix representing a linear map from an orthonormal basis {ei}\{e_i\}{ei​} to {Aei}\{Ae_i\}{Aei​}, then the set {Aei}\{Ae_i\}{Aei​} is also orthonormal.

Proof. As previously observed, the columns of a matrix representing a linear map are Ae1,Ae2,...,AenAe_1, Ae_2, ..., Ae_nAe1​,Ae2​,...,Aen​. As the columns of an orthogonal matrix are orthonormal, the set {Aei}\{Ae_i\}{Aei​} must also be orthonormal.

Examples of orthonormal matrices include the rotation matrix and the reflection matrix, given in the last section (Examples of Linear Maps).
