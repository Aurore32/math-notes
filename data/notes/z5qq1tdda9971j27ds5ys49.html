To paraphrase almost verbatim from my textbook, this is "one of these theorems which seem deep and mysterious until you realize all it's telling you is that you can get things in a list into some order you want by swapping these things around two at a time." The sign of a transposition

Definition. The well-definedness of #(σ)\#(\sigma)#(σ) is predicated on the following theorem:

Theorem. The decomposition of a permutation into transpositions is, in general, not unique; however, its sign is. The number of transpositions present in the transposition decomposition of a permutation σ∈Sn\sigma \in S_nσ∈Sn​ will either be always odd or always even, yielding either 111 (in the even case) or −1-1−1 (the odd case) as the sign of σ\sigmaσ across all possible decompositions.

Alternating groups

The well-definedness of the sign of a permutation σ\sigmaσ, written sign(σ)\text{sign}(\sigma)sign(σ), allows us to classify permutations into two types based on their sign.

Definition. Call σ\sigmaσ an even permutation if sign(σ)=1\text{sign}(\sigma) = 1sign(σ)=1; call σ\sigmaσ an odd permutation if instead sign(σ)=−1\text{sign}(\sigma) = -1sign(σ)=−1. (Note the similarity to the alternating Levi-Civita symbol ϵ...\epsilon_{...}ϵ...​!)

More interesting still is

Theorem. sign(σ)\text{sign}(\sigma)sign(σ) for σ∈Sn\sigma \in S_nσ∈Sn​ is a group homomorphism from SnS_nSn​ to the cyclic group C2={−1,1}C_2 = \{-1, 1\}C2​={−1,1}.

Proof.

As multiplication is the binary operation common to both sets, our condition for a group homomorphism is

sign(στ)=sign(σ)sign(τ)\text{sign}(\sigma \tau) = \text{sign}(\sigma) \text{sign}(\tau)sign(στ)=sign(σ)sign(τ)

for any two permutations σ,τ∈Sn\sigma, \tau \in S_nσ,τ∈Sn​. Suppose that σ\sigmaσ is composed of kkk transpositions, and τ\tauτ of lll; by the well-definedness of the sign function, we know that as kkk and lll are constant mod 2\text{mod }2mod 2 no matter the decomposition used, k+lk + lk+l will also be constant mod 2\text{mod }2mod 2 - exactly the number of transpositions making up στ\sigma \tauστ (of course, this is not the only decomposition possible for στ\sigma \tauστ). This leads to

sign(στ)=(−1)k+l=(−1)k(−1)l=sign(σ)sign(τ)\text{sign}(\sigma \tau) = (-1)^{k+l}=(-1)^k (-1)^l = \text{sign}(\sigma) \text{sign}(\tau)sign(στ)=(−1)k+l=(−1)k(−1)l=sign(σ)sign(τ)

satisfying the condition for a homomorphism. □\square□

This leads directly to

Definition. The alternating group AnA_nAn​ consists of all permutations in SnS_nSn​ that have sign 111 (or, equivalently, are even).

Why define such a group - which, by the way, we haven't even shown to be a group yet - at all? There are probably many, many reasons, the vast majority of which I have no hopes of comprehending or communicating, but two stand out as most immediate:

  1. Theorem. AnA_nAn​ is a subgroup of SnS_nSn​; the set of odd permutations in SnS_nSn​ is not.

    This follows from the fact that 1) every permutation has an inverse, including even ones, and inverted even permutations are still even by virtue of the identity permutation eee being even; and 2) two even permutations composed together are still even (as sign)\text{sign})sign) is a multiplicative function). The same is not true for odd permutations; the identity permutation is not odd, and the product of two odd permutations is even (−1⋅−1=1-1 \cdot -1 = 1−1⋅−1=1.)

  2. Even permutations preserve orientation: if xxx, yyy and zzz were the coordinate axes with right-handed orientation, and σ\sigmaσ was an even permutation on these axes, then the resulting set of axes would remain right-handed. Importantly, this finds an application both in the formula for the cross product

    a×b=ϵijkajbk\mathbf{a \times b} = \epsilon_{ijk}a_jb_ka×b=ϵijk​aj​bk​

    where ϵ\epsilonϵ is just the sign function in a trench coat after several cosmetic surgeries in Korea, as well as for the determinant

    det⁡A=∑σsign(σ)aσ(i)i.\det A = \sum_{\sigma}\text{sign}(\sigma)a_{\sigma(i) i}.detA=σ∑​sign(σ)aσ(i)i​.

One last result to cap things off! The sign of a permutation is the product of the signs of the cycles in its disjoint cycle decomposition. This much we know already, but we further propose that

Proposition. The sign of any kkk-cycle (a1a2...ak)(a_1 a_2 ... a_k)(a1​a2​...ak​) is (−1)k−1(-1)^{k-1}(−1)k−1. This directly follows from (a1a2...ak)=(a1a2)(a2a3)...(ak−1ak)(a_1a_2... a_k) =(a_1 a_2)(a_2 a_3) ... (a_{k-1} a_k)(a1​a2​...ak​)=(a1​a2​)(a2​a3​)...(ak−1​ak​) totaling k−1k-1k−1 transpositions.

As such, if kkk is even, then the kkk-cycle is odd; if kkk is odd, then the kkk-cycle is even. Fantastic.

This results in

Proposition. A permutation is even if there is an even number of odd cycles in its disjoint cycle decomposition, per the above.

which amounts to a fairly convenient way to check the sign of any permutation, all things considered.
