Matrix Inverses and Linear Equations

Systems of 2×22\times 22×2 equations

Consider a general system of linear equations in two variables x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​:

{A11x1+A12x2=d1A21x1+A22x2=d2\begin{cases} A_{11} x_1 + A_{12} x_2 = d_1 \\ A_{21} x_1 + A_{22} x_2 = d_2 \end{cases}{A11​x1​+A12​x2​=d1​A21​x1​+A22​x2​=d2​​

This can be rewritten in matrix form as

Ax=dA\mathbf{x=d}Ax=d

where column vectors x=[x1x2]\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{bmatrix}x=[x1​x2​​] and d=[d1d2]\mathbf{d}=\begin{bmatrix}d_1\\d_2\end{bmatrix}d=[d1​d2​​]. The general solution of this system can arise from substitution, e.g. writing x2=d1−A11x1A12x_2 = \frac{d_1-A_{11}x_1}{A_{12}}x2​=A12​d1​−A11​x1​​ and substituting into the second equation:

x=[A22d1−A12d2det⁡A−A21d1+A11d2det⁡A]=1det⁡A[A22d1−A12d2−A21d1+A11d2]=1det⁡A[A22−A12−A21A11][d1d2]=1det⁡A[A22−A12−A21A11]d\begin{aligned} \mathbf{x} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{A_{22}d_1 - A_{12}d_2}{\det A} \\ \frac{- A_{21}d_1 + A_{11}d_2}{\det A} \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \frac{1}{\det A}\begin{bmatrix} A_{22}d_1 - A_{12}d_2 \\ - A_{21}d_1 + A_{11}d_2 \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \frac{1}{\det A} \begin{bmatrix} A_{22} &- A_{12} \\ - A_{21} & A_{11} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} d_1 \\ d_2 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\det A} \begin{bmatrix} A_{22} &- A_{12} \\ - A_{21} & A_{11} \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{d} \end{aligned}x​=[detAA22​d1​−A12​d2​​detA−A21​d1​+A11​d2​​​]=detA1​[A22​d1​−A12​d2​−A21​d1​+A11​d2​​]=detA1​[A22​−A21​​−A12​A11​​][d1​d2​​]=detA1​[A22​−A21​​−A12​A11​​]d​

Theorem. The inverse of a 2×22 \times 22×2 matrix AAA is given generally by:

A−1=1det⁡A[A22−A12−A21A11].A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \begin{bmatrix} A_{22} &- A_{12} \\ - A_{21} & A_{11} \end{bmatrix}.A−1=detA1​[A22​−A21​​−A12​A11​​].

Inverse of a general n×nn\times nn×n matrix

Recall the Laplace expansion formulae for determinants of an n×nn\times nn×n matrix, A={Aij}A=\{A_{ij}\}A={Aij​}:

det⁡A=∑jAijΔij=∑iAijΔij\det A = \sum_{j}A_{ij}\Delta_{ij} = \sum_{i}A_{ij}\Delta_{ij}detA=j∑​Aij​Δij​=i∑​Aij​Δij​

Now suppose that we replace the IIIth row of AAA with the iiith row of AAA, I≠iI\neq iI=i, such that we create a new matrix A′A'A′ with two identical rows (row III = row iii). Per a previously proven property, the matrix A′A'A′ has zero determinant as it has two identical rows; however, the cofactors ΔIj\Delta_{Ij}ΔIj​ will remain unchanged as they involve the rest of the matrix aside from row III, which were not affected. Using row III to take the determinant via the Laplace expansion formula gives

∑jAIj′ΔIj=∑jAijΔIj=0\sum_{j}A'_{Ij}\Delta_{Ij}=\sum_{j}A_{ij}\Delta_{Ij}=0j∑​AIj′​ΔIj​=j∑​Aij​ΔIj​=0

Swapping the IIIth column of AAA with the iiith column produces an identical result:

∑jAjI′ΔjI=∑jAjiΔjI=0\sum_{j}A'_{jI}\Delta_{jI}=\sum_{j}A_{ji}\Delta_{jI}=0j∑​AjI′​ΔjI​=j∑​Aji​ΔjI​=0

Thus we have, for any III and using suffix notation:

{AjiΔjI=0AijΔIj=0\begin{cases} A_{ji}\Delta_{jI}=0 \\ A_{ij}\Delta_{Ij}=0 \end{cases}{Aji​ΔjI​=0Aij​ΔIj​=0​

if i≠Ii \neq Ii=I. However, if i=Ii = Ii=I, nothing has been swapped and we simply have

{AjiΔjI=det⁡AAijΔIj=det⁡A\begin{cases} A_{ji}\Delta_{jI}=\det A \\ A_{ij}\Delta_{Ij}=\det A \end{cases}{Aji​ΔjI​=detAAij​ΔIj​=detA​

by Laplace expansion; alternatively, using the Kronecker delta, we have

Lemma.

{AjiΔjI=δiIdet⁡A, summing over jAijΔIj=δiIdet⁡A, summing over j\begin{cases} A_{ji}\Delta_{jI}=\delta_{iI}\det A, \text{ summing over $j$} \\ A_{ij}\Delta_{Ij}=\delta_{iI}\det A, \text{ summing over $j$} \end{cases}{Aji​ΔjI​=δiI​detA, summing over jAij​ΔIj​=δiI​detA, summing over j​

This leads finally to

Theorem. General inverse for n×nn\times nn×n matrices. For a n×nn\times nn×n square matrix AAA with det⁡A≠0\det A \neq 0detA=0, its inverse is given by

A−1=1det⁡A{Δji}=1det⁡A[Δ11Δ12...Δ1nΔ21Δ22...Δ2n⋮⋮⋱⋮Δn1Δn2...Δnn]TA^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A}\{\Delta_{ji}\} = \frac{1}{\det A} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta_{11} & \Delta_{12} & ... & \Delta_{1n} \\ \Delta_{21} & \Delta_{22} & ... & \Delta_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \Delta_{n1} & \Delta_{n2} & ... & \Delta_{nn} \end{bmatrix}^TA−1=detA1​{Δji​}=detA1​⎣⎡​Δ11​Δ21​⋮Δn1​​Δ12​Δ22​⋮Δn2​​......⋱...​Δ1n​Δ2n​⋮Δnn​​⎦⎤​T

(Note the tiny little transpose there!)

We assert that A−1A=AA−1=IA^{-1} A = AA^{-1} = IA−1A=AA−1=I.

Proof.

A−1A=1det⁡AΔikAjk=det⁡Adet⁡Aδij by the above lemma=δij, matching the identity matrixAA−1=1det⁡AAikΔjk=det⁡Adet⁡Aδij by the above lemma=δij, matching the identity matrix\begin{aligned} A^{-1}A &= \frac{1}{\det A} \Delta_{ik}A_{jk} \\ &= \frac{\det A}{\det A} \delta_{ij} \text{ by the above lemma} \\ &= \delta_{ij}, \text{ matching the identity matrix} \\ \\ AA^{-1} &= \frac{1}{\det A} A_{ik}\Delta_{jk} \\ &= \frac{\det A}{\det A} \delta_{ij} \text{ by the above lemma} \\ &= \delta_{ij}, \text{ matching the identity matrix} \\ \\ \end{aligned}A−1AAA−1​=detA1​Δik​Ajk​=detAdetA​δij​ by the above lemma=δij​, matching the identity matrix=detA1​Aik​Δjk​=detAdetA​δij​ by the above lemma=δij​, matching the identity matrix​

as AA−1=IAA^{-1} = IAA−1=I and A−1A=IA^{-1}A=IA−1A=I, A−1A^{-1}A−1 is by definition the inverse of III. (A little on-the-nose, maybe, but it's true!)

Essentially, this tells us that taking an inverse of a n×nn\times nn×n matrix is as easy as listening to Mozart sped up in your left ear while listening to the Chinese version of the Bible in reverse in your right: all you need to do is to form a matrix of cofactors Δ\DeltaΔ from AAA by taking the cofactor of each term and leaving it there, then transpose it, then dividing it by det⁡A\det AdetA. (What? Wrong linear equations?)

Okay, so never mind. Let's take a look at the above 2×22\times 22×2 example of systems of linear equations, and see how we can generalize it:

Ax=dA\mathbf{x=d}Ax=d

This forms a valid system of nnn equations in nnn variables if AAA is n×nn\times nn×n, x\mathbf{x}x is n×1n \times 1n×1, and d\mathbf{d}d is n×1n \times 1n×1.

Definition. If d\mathbf{d}d is the zero vector, the system of equations is deemed homogeneous; otherwise, it is inhomogeneous.

As demonstrated, linear algebra leads to

x=A−1d\mathbf{x=}A^{-1}\mathbf{d}x=A−1d

if the system is inhomogeneous. Trying to calculate the solution vector x\mathbf{x}x through directly calculating the inverse of AAA would lead to a very tired hand and a thoroughly punched calculator. Exactly how punched? Consider just the step of taking det⁡A\det AdetA: using Laplace expansion, det⁡A\det AdetA can be written as a sum of nnn determinants of (n−1)×(n−1)(n-1)\times (n-1)(n−1)×(n−1) matrices (cofactors of AAA), which, in turn, can each be written as a sum of (n−1)(n-1)(n−1) determinants of (n−2)×(n−2)(n-2) \times (n-2)(n−2)×(n−2) matrices, etc.

In other words, if fnf_nfn​ represents the number of operations required to calculate det⁡A\det AdetA, the we have

fn=nfn−1+2n−1f_n = n f_{n-1} + 2n - 1fn​=nfn−1​+2n−1

recursively, as an additional n−1n-1n−1 additions and nnn multiplications must be made between the determinants. This leads to the O(n⋅n!)O(n\cdot n!)O(n⋅n!) time-complexity mentioned above - a shudderingly-large number, one that must not be disturbed with a ten-kilometer iron pole while wearing ten hazmat suits and a blast jacket.

Gaussian elimination

We're going to throw matrices into the trash bin like the pieces of scrapyard junk they are; they're cramping our style. Instead, we're going back to treating systems of equations like systems of equations:

{A11x1+A12x2+...+A1nxn=d1A21x1+A22x2+...+A2nxn=d2⋮Am1x1+Am2x2+...+Amnxn=dm\begin{cases} A_{11}x_1 + A_{12}x_2 + ... + A_{1n}x_n = d_1 \\ A_{21} x_1 + A_{22} x_2 + ... + A_{2n} x_n = d_2 \\ \vdots \\ A_{m1} x_1 + A_{m2} x_2 + ... + A_{mn} x_n = d_m \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​A11​x1​+A12​x2​+...+A1n​xn​=d1​A21​x1​+A22​x2​+...+A2n​xn​=d2​⋮Am1​x1​+Am2​x2​+...+Amn​xn​=dm​​

(Note that this is a system of mmm equations with nnn variables, where mmm need not equal nnn).

Really, who needs matrices when you have thirty minutes of free time and a truly messed-up fetish for writing the letter xxx?

The process is familiar. First insist that A11A_{11}A11​, the leading coefficient, is nonzero; if it is, reorder the rows such that the first row has a nonzero leading coefficient. If that isn't possible, i.e. every row has a zero leading coefficient, blow your piece of paper up with a homemade nuke relabel x2,...,xnx_2, ..., x_nx2​,...,xn​ to x1,...,xn−1x_1, ..., x_{n-1}x1​,...,xn−1​ and start over.

We begin by eliminating a single variable, e.g. x1x_1x1​. In order to erase every trace of x1x_1x1​ from the miserable existence of this prison we call a system, we will need to subtract every other row by a certain multiple of the first row: if row 1 is denoted (1)=A11x1+A12x2+...+A1nxn=d1(1) = A_{11}x_1 + A_{12}x_2 + ... + A_{1n}x_n = d_1(1)=A11​x1​+A12​x2​+...+A1n​xn​=d1​, row 2 is denoted (2)=A21x1+A22x2+...+A2nxn=d2(2) = A_{21}x_1 + A_{22}x_2 + ... + A_{2n}x_n = d_2(2)=A21​x1​+A22​x2​+...+A2n​xn​=d2​ and so on, then (2)−A21A11(1)(2) - \frac{A_{21}}{A_{11}}(1)(2)−A11​A21​​(1) eliminates x1x_1x1​ from (2)(2)(2), (3)−A31A11(1)(3)-\frac{A_{31}}{A_{11}}(1)(3)−A11​A31​​(1) eliminates x1x_1x1​ from (3)(3)(3), ..., (m)−Am1A11(1)(m)-\frac{A_{m1}}{A_{11}}(1)(m)−A11​Am1​​(1) eliminates x1x_1x1​ from (m)(m)(m). We are thus left with the system

{(1),(2)−A21A11(1),⋮(m)−Am1A11(1).\begin{cases} (1), \\ (2) - \frac{A_{21}}{A_{11}}(1), \\ \vdots \\ (m) - \frac{A_{m1}}{A_{11}}(1). \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​(1),(2)−A11​A21​​(1),⋮(m)−A11​Am1​​(1).​

This system does not involve x1x_1x1​ in any equation except the first. And now we do it again; first insist that the leading coefficient of x2x_2x2​ is nonzero (and take the necessary measures to ensure it is so), then eliminate x2x_2x2​ from every equation below the second (the third, the fourth, etc.), and so on - where, after x2x_2x2​ is eliminated, only the first and second equations will contain x2x_2x2​. And so will only the first three equations contain x3x_3x3​, the first four x4x_4x4​, etc. etc. This leaves us with a system of the form

{A11x1+A12x2+...+A1nxn=d10x1+A22′x2+...+A2n′xn=d2′0x1+0x2+A33′x3+...+A3n′xn=d3′0x1+0x2+0x3+A44′x4+...+A4n′xn=d4′⋮0x1+0x2+0x3+...+Amn′xm=dm′\begin{cases} A_{11}x_1 + A_{12}x_2 + ... + A_{1n}x_n = d_1 \\ 0x_1 +A_{22}'x_2 + ... + A_{2n}'x_n = d_2' \\ 0x_1 + 0x_2 + A_{33}'x_3 + ... + A_{3n}'x_n = d_3' \\ 0x_1 + 0x_2 + 0x_3 + A_{44}'x_4 + ... + A_{4n}'x_n = d_4' \\ \vdots \\ 0x_1 + 0x_2 + 0x_3 + ... + A_{mn}'x_m = d_m' \\ \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​A11​x1​+A12​x2​+...+A1n​xn​=d1​0x1​+A22′​x2​+...+A2n′​xn​=d2′​0x1​+0x2​+A33′​x3​+...+A3n′​xn​=d3′​0x1​+0x2​+0x3​+A44′​x4​+...+A4n′​xn​=d4′​⋮0x1​+0x2​+0x3​+...+Amn′​xm​=dm′​​

where d′d'd′ and A′A'A′ denote the remaining coefficients after elimination. Note that every subsequent row will have less variables than the last; thus, if a row is zero on the left-hand side, all the next rows will be zero as well. There are now three possible cases for the system.

  1. The overdetermined case. If there exists an r<nr < nr<n such that the left-hand side of equation (r)(r)(r) is zero and the right-hand side dr′≠0d_r' \neq 0dr′​=0, then the system is inconsistent and there are no solutions.
  2. Unique solution. If there only exists an r=n+1r = n+1r=n+1 such that the left-hand side of equation (r)(r)(r) is zero (i.e. the previous nnn equations are nonzero, and only from the n+1n+1n+1th equation onwards are the equations zero), and none of dn+1′,...,dm′d'_{n+1}, ..., d'_{m}dn+1′​,...,dm′​ are zero, then there is a unique solution that can be found by taking the value of xnx_nxn​ and substituting into equation (n−1)(n-1)(n−1), then taking xn−1x_{n-1}xn−1​ and substituting into (n−2)(n-2)(n−2), etc.
  3. Infinite solutions. If there exists an r<nr<nr<n such that the left-hand side of equation (r)(r)(r) is zero and all of dr′,dr+1′,...,dm′d'_r, d'_{r+1}, ..., d'_mdr′​,dr+1′​,...,dm′​ are zero, then there are no inconsistencies in the system, but there aren't enough equations to uniquely determine a single solution.

Remarks.

  1. It can be convenient to reorder the equations such that A11A_{11}A11​ has the maximum absolute value of (A11,A21,...,An1)(A_{11},A_{21},...,A_{n1})(A11​,A21​,...,An1​).
  2. Gaussian elimination can be achieved in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) - much more feasible than finding matrix inverses; however, algorithms also exist to find determinants, and thus inverses, in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) (which are based on Gaussian elimination).
  3. A linear system is consistent if it has at least one solution, and inconsistent if it has none.
  4. The above Gaussian elimination algorithm involves the following elementary row operations:
    • Swapping two rows
    • Adding a constant multiple of one row to another
    • Multiplying a row by a scalar constant
  5. Two systems of linear equations are row-equivalent if they can each be obtained from elementary row operations of the other; these systems have the same solutions.
  6. Swapping two rows and adding a constant multiple of one row to another, alongside swapping two columns, change the determinant of the system's associated matrix by only a sign (as previously shown). Thus, performing Gaussian elimination on a matrix and transforming it into
A′=[A11A12A13...A1n0A22′A23′...A2n′00A33′...A3n′⋮⋮⋮⋱⋮000...Ann′]A' = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & A_{13} & ... & A_{1n} \\ 0 & A'_{22} & A'_{23} & ... & A'_{2n} \\ 0 & 0 & A'_{33} & ... & A'_{3n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & ... & A'_{nn} \end{bmatrix}A′=⎣⎡​A11​00⋮0​A12​A22′​0⋮0​A13​A23′​A33′​⋮0​.........⋱...​A1n​A2n′​A3n′​⋮Ann′​​⎦⎤​

with det⁡A′=(−1)kdet⁡A\det A' = (-1)^k \det AdetA′=(−1)kdetA where kkk is the number of column and row swaps performed, leads to det⁡A′=A11A22′A33′...Ann′\det A' = A_{11}A'_{22}A'_{33}...A'_{nn}detA′=A11​A22′​A33′​...Ann′​ (using the first column for Laplace expansion). As Gaussian elimination can be achieved in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3), the determinant can also be found in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) (as what remains is simply a multiplication of the diagonal elements).
